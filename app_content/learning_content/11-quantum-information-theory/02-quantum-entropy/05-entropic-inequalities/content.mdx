## 1. The Entropy Cone

The set of achievable entropy vectors forms a cone:

<Definition term="Quantum Entropy Cone">
For n parties, the quantum entropy cone is:
$$\Gamma_n^* = \{(S_A)_{A \subseteq [n]} : \exists \rho \text{ such that } S_A = S(\rho_A)\}$$
</Definition>

For 2 parties, the cone is fully characterized by:
- Non-negativity: $S_A \geq 0$
- Subadditivity: $S_{AB} \leq S_A + S_B$
- Araki-Lieb: $|S_A - S_B| \leq S_{AB}$

For 3+ parties, additional constraints exist beyond SSA.

## 2. Beyond Strong Subadditivity

### Weak Monotonicity
$$S(AB) + S(BC) \geq S(B) + S(ABC)$$

### Ingleton-like Inequalities (Classical)
Classical entropy satisfies additional constraints not obeyed by quantum entropy.

### Constrained Inequalities
With constraints (e.g., pure global state), additional inequalities hold.

## 3. Quantum vs Classical

Key differences:
- Quantum conditional entropy can be negative
- Quantum mutual information can exceed classical bounds
- Some classical inequalities fail quantumly

## Summary

Entropic inequalities form a rich mathematical structure constraining quantum correlations. SSA is fundamental, but the complete picture involves entropy cones and their characterization.
