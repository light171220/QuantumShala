## 1. Introduction to Photonic Quantum Computing

Photons are nature's ideal carriers of quantum information. They travel at the speed of light, exhibit minimal interaction with the environment (leading to long coherence times), and can be easily transmitted through optical fibers or free space for quantum communication.

<Definition term="Photonic Qubit">
A photonic qubit is a two-level quantum system encoded in a single photon's degrees of freedom, such as polarization, spatial path, time-bin, or orbital angular momentum.
</Definition>

### Why Photons for Quantum Computing?

| Advantage | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Long coherence | Minimal decoherence at room temperature |
| Speed | Information travels at the speed of light |
| Connectivity | Natural for quantum networks and communication |
| Room temperature | No cryogenic cooling required for qubits |

<Tip>
Photons are the only practical carriers for quantum communication over long distances. This makes photonic quantum computing naturally compatible with the quantum internet.
</Tip>

## 2. Quantum Description of Single Photons

### The Photon Wavefunction

A single photon in a specific mode can be described using the creation operator $\hat{a}^\dagger$ acting on the vacuum state:

<Formula>
$$|1\rangle = \hat{a}^\dagger |0\rangle$$
</Formula>

where $|0\rangle$ is the vacuum state (no photons) and $|1\rangle$ represents exactly one photon in that mode.

### Photon Number States

The quantum electromagnetic field supports **Fock states** (number states) $|n\rangle$ representing exactly $n$ photons:

<Formula>
$$|n\rangle = \frac{(\hat{a}^\dagger)^n}{\sqrt{n!}}|0\rangle$$
</Formula>

For quantum computing, we work primarily with single photons ($n=1$), which serve as flying qubits.

### Photon Energy and Wavelength

A single photon carries energy proportional to its frequency:

<Formula>
$$E = h\nu = \hbar\omega = \frac{hc}{\lambda}$$
</Formula>

Common wavelengths for photonic qubits:
- **780 nm**: Rubidium atomic transitions (atom-photon interfaces)
- **850 nm**: Silicon photodetectors optimal range
- **1310 nm**: Telecom O-band (zero dispersion in fiber)
- **1550 nm**: Telecom C-band (minimum loss in fiber)

## 3. Polarization Encoding

The most intuitive and widely used photonic qubit encoding uses the polarization degree of freedom.

### Polarization States

Light polarization describes the orientation of the electric field oscillation. For a photon traveling along the z-axis:

<Definition term="Polarization Qubit">
A polarization qubit encodes quantum information in the polarization state of a single photon, with horizontal (H) and vertical (V) polarizations forming the computational basis.
</Definition>

The computational basis states are:

<Formula>
$$|0\rangle \equiv |H\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle \equiv |V\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
</Formula>

### Jones Vector Formalism

The **Jones vector** describes the polarization state of light:

<Formula>
$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|H\rangle + \beta|V\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix}$$
</Formula>

where $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$ for a normalized state.

### Important Polarization States

| State | Symbol | Jones Vector | Description |
|-------|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Horizontal | $\|H\rangle$ | $(1, 0)^T$ | Computational $\|0\rangle$ |
| Vertical | $\|V\rangle$ | $(0, 1)^T$ | Computational $\|1\rangle$ |
| Diagonal | $\|D\rangle$ | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1)^T$ | $\|+\rangle$ state |
| Anti-diagonal | $\|A\rangle$ | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, -1)^T$ | $\|-\rangle$ state |
| Right circular | $\|R\rangle$ | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, i)^T$ | $\|+i\rangle$ state |
| Left circular | $\|L\rangle$ | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, -i)^T$ | $\|-i\rangle$ state |

```python
import numpy as np

# Define polarization states as Jones vectors
H = np.array([1, 0], dtype=complex)  # Horizontal
V = np.array([0, 1], dtype=complex)  # Vertical
D = np.array([1, 1], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)  # Diagonal
A = np.array([1, -1], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)  # Anti-diagonal
R = np.array([1, 1j], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)  # Right circular
L = np.array([1, -1j], dtype=complex) / np.sqrt(2)  # Left circular

# Verify orthogonality of basis pairs
print(f"<H|V> = {np.vdot(H, V)}")  # Should be 0
print(f"<D|A> = {np.vdot(D, A)}")  # Should be 0
print(f"<R|L> = {np.vdot(R, L)}")  # Should be 0

# Express D in H/V basis
print(f"|D> = {np.vdot(H, D):.3f}|H> + {np.vdot(V, D):.3f}|V>")
```

## 4. Bloch Sphere for Polarization

The polarization state of a photon maps naturally onto the Bloch sphere, which in this context is called the **Poincare sphere**:

<Formula>
$$|\psi\rangle = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}|H\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}|V\rangle$$
</Formula>

The mapping to the Bloch sphere:
- **North pole** ($\theta=0$): $|H\rangle$
- **South pole** ($\theta=\pi$): $|V\rangle$
- **Equator**: Equal superpositions (D, A, R, L states)

<Tip>
The three mutually unbiased bases (H/V, D/A, R/L) correspond to measurements along the three Cartesian axes of the Poincare sphere.
</Tip>

## 5. Path (Dual-Rail) Encoding

An alternative to polarization encoding uses the photon's spatial path.

<Definition term="Dual-Rail Encoding">
In dual-rail encoding, a qubit is represented by a single photon that can exist in one of two spatial modes (paths), typically labeled as upper (U) and lower (L) arms of an interferometer.
</Definition>

<Formula>
$$|0\rangle \equiv |1_U, 0_L\rangle, \quad |1\rangle \equiv |0_U, 1_L\rangle$$
</Formula>

The notation $|n_U, m_L\rangle$ indicates $n$ photons in the upper path and $m$ photons in the lower path.

### Advantages of Path Encoding

1. **High-fidelity operations**: Phase shifters have very low loss
2. **Scalability**: Compatible with integrated photonic circuits
3. **Stability**: Less sensitive to polarization drift in fibers

### Conversion Between Encodings

A **polarizing beam splitter (PBS)** converts between polarization and path encoding:

```python
import numpy as np

def polarization_to_path(polarization_state):
    """
    PBS converts polarization encoding to path encoding.
    |H> -> photon in transmission port (|0>_path)
    |V> -> photon in reflection port (|1>_path)
    """
    # PBS is essentially identity in this basis
    return polarization_state

def path_to_polarization(path_state, hwp_angle=np.pi/4):
    """
    Recombining paths through a PBS converts back to polarization.
    With appropriate waveplates, any conversion is possible.
    """
    return path_state
```

## 6. Time-Bin Encoding

For fiber-based quantum communication, **time-bin encoding** offers excellent stability.

<Definition term="Time-Bin Qubit">
A time-bin qubit encodes information in whether a photon arrives in an early or late time window, typically created using an unbalanced interferometer.
</Definition>

<Formula>
$$|0\rangle \equiv |\text{early}\rangle, \quad |1\rangle \equiv |\text{late}\rangle$$
</Formula>

A superposition state corresponds to the photon being in a coherent superposition of arrival times:

<Formula>
$$|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\text{early}\rangle + e^{i\phi}\beta|\text{late}\rangle$$
</Formula>

### Creating Time-Bin Qubits

An unbalanced Mach-Zehnder interferometer creates time-bin superpositions:

```python
import numpy as np

def time_bin_interferometer(input_state, path_difference_m, wavelength_m):
    """
    Model an unbalanced interferometer for time-bin encoding.

    Args:
        input_state: Input polarization/path state
        path_difference_m: Path length difference in meters
        wavelength_m: Photon wavelength in meters

    Returns:
        Phase acquired between time bins
    """
    # Phase difference due to path length difference
    phase = 2 * np.pi * path_difference_m / wavelength_m

    # Time difference between early and late bins
    c = 3e8  # Speed of light
    time_difference_s = path_difference_m / c

    return {
        'phase': phase,
        'time_difference_ns': time_difference_s * 1e9
    }

# Example: 1 meter path difference at 1550 nm
result = time_bin_interferometer(None, 1.0, 1550e-9)
print(f"Time difference: {result['time_difference_ns']:.2f} ns")
```

<Warning>
Time-bin qubits require careful stabilization of the interferometer path length. A 1 nm path length change at 1550 nm wavelength causes a phase shift of about 0.004 radians.
</Warning>

## 7. Other Encoding Schemes

### Orbital Angular Momentum (OAM)

Photons can carry orbital angular momentum with discrete values $\ell\hbar$:

<Formula>
$$|\psi\rangle = \sum_\ell c_\ell |\ell\rangle$$
</Formula>

OAM provides a natural high-dimensional encoding, but is challenging to work with in fiber systems.

### Frequency-Bin Encoding

The photon's frequency can encode quantum information:

<Formula>
$$|0\rangle \equiv |\omega_1\rangle, \quad |1\rangle \equiv |\omega_2\rangle$$
</Formula>

This is compatible with wavelength-division multiplexing technology in telecommunications.

## 8. Comparison of Encoding Schemes

| Encoding | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|----------|------|------|----------|
| Polarization | Intuitive, easy manipulation | Unstable in long fibers | Free-space, short fiber |
| Path (dual-rail) | Stable, good for integration | Requires two spatial modes | Integrated photonics |
| Time-bin | Very stable in fiber | Requires fast timing | Long-distance fiber QKD |
| OAM | High-dimensional | Difficult in fiber | Free-space, lab experiments |
| Frequency | Telecom compatible | Complex manipulation | Wavelength multiplexing |

## 9. Single-Photon Interference

The quintessential quantum behavior of photons is **single-photon interference**.

### The Mach-Zehnder Interferometer

A single photon entering a Mach-Zehnder interferometer exhibits interference:

<Formula>
$$P(\text{detector 1}) = \cos^2\frac{\phi}{2}, \quad P(\text{detector 2}) = \sin^2\frac{\phi}{2}$$
</Formula>

where $\phi$ is the relative phase between the two paths.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def mach_zehnder_output(phase_difference):
    """
    Calculate output probabilities for a Mach-Zehnder interferometer.

    Args:
        phase_difference: Phase difference between paths (radians)

    Returns:
        Probabilities for detectors 1 and 2
    """
    p1 = np.cos(phase_difference / 2)**2
    p2 = np.sin(phase_difference / 2)**2
    return p1, p2

# Scan phase and plot
phases = np.linspace(0, 4*np.pi, 200)
p1_values = [mach_zehnder_output(phi)[0] for phi in phases]
p2_values = [mach_zehnder_output(phi)[1] for phi in phases]

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.plot(phases/np.pi, p1_values, label='Detector 1')
plt.plot(phases/np.pi, p2_values, label='Detector 2')
plt.xlabel('Phase difference (units of pi)')
plt.ylabel('Detection probability')
plt.title('Single-Photon Interference in Mach-Zehnder Interferometer')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()
```

<Tip>
This interference occurs even when only one photon is in the interferometer at a time - the photon interferes with itself! This is a purely quantum mechanical effect with no classical analog.
</Tip>

## 10. Advantages and Challenges of Photonic Qubits

### Advantages

1. **Negligible decoherence**: Photons interact weakly with the environment
2. **Room temperature operation**: No dilution refrigerators needed
3. **High-speed operations**: Limited only by the speed of light
4. **Natural for networking**: Photons are already used for communication
5. **Wavelength flexibility**: Can match atomic transitions or telecom bands

### Challenges

1. **Weak photon-photon interaction**: Makes two-qubit gates probabilistic
2. **Photon loss**: Fiber attenuation, coupling losses, detector inefficiency
3. **Single-photon generation**: Deterministic sources are still challenging
4. **Detection efficiency**: Must approach near-unity for scalability

<Warning>
The lack of direct photon-photon interaction is the fundamental challenge of photonic quantum computing. The KLM scheme (covered in the next lesson) overcomes this using measurement-induced nonlinearity, but at the cost of probabilistic gates.
</Warning>

## 11. Summary

Photonic qubits offer unique advantages for quantum information processing:

1. **Single photons** provide natural two-level systems via various degrees of freedom
2. **Polarization encoding** is intuitive and maps directly to the Bloch sphere
3. **Path encoding** is ideal for integrated photonic circuits
4. **Time-bin encoding** provides stability for fiber-based communication
5. **Encoding conversions** are straightforward using standard optical elements

The main challenge - weak photon-photon interactions - can be overcome using measurement-induced nonlinearity, as we will explore in the next lesson on linear optical gates.
