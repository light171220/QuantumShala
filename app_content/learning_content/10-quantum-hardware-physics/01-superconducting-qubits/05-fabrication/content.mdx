## 1. Overview of Qubit Fabrication

Superconducting qubit fabrication combines thin-film deposition, nanolithography, and precision materials engineering. The process must achieve:

- **Nanometer-scale precision**: Josephson junctions are ~100 nm
- **High material quality**: Surface defects cause decoherence
- **Reproducibility**: Qubit frequencies must be predictable
- **Scalability**: Large arrays require consistent fabrication

<Definition term="Josephson Junction">
A Josephson junction in a superconducting qubit is typically a trilayer structure of aluminum (Al), aluminum oxide (AlOx), and aluminum, with the oxide barrier ~1 nm thick, allowing Cooper pair tunneling.
</Definition>

## 2. Substrate and Materials

### Substrate Choices

| Substrate | Advantages | Challenges |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| Silicon | Low loss, standard | Native oxide TLS |
| Sapphire | Very low loss | Expensive, harder processing |
| Silicon on insulator | Good isolation | More complex |

### Superconducting Materials

**Aluminum (Al)** is the most common choice:
- Critical temperature: $T_c \approx 1.2$ K
- Native oxide forms naturally (self-limiting)
- Well-characterized junction properties
- Gap: $\Delta \approx 180$ $\mu$eV

**Other materials** for specialized applications:
- Niobium (Nb): Higher $T_c$ (~9 K), used for resonators
- Tantalum (Ta): Lower TLS loss, emerging material
- Titanium nitride (TiN): Kinetic inductance applications

## 3. The Dolan Bridge Technique

The **Dolan bridge** (or shadow evaporation) technique is the standard method for creating Al/AlOx/Al Josephson junctions.

### Process Steps

1. **Resist coating**: Bilayer resist (e.g., MMA/PMMA)
2. **Lithography**: E-beam exposes junction pattern
3. **Development**: Creates undercut profile
4. **First evaporation**: Al at angle θ₁
5. **Oxidation**: Controlled O₂ exposure forms AlOx
6. **Second evaporation**: Al at angle θ₂ (opposite direction)
7. **Liftoff**: Remove resist, leaving junction

### Junction Geometry

<Formula>
$$A_J = w \times l \times \frac{\sin\theta_1 + \sin\theta_2}{\cos\theta_1}$$
</Formula>

The junction area determines $I_c$ and thus $E_J$:

<Formula>
$$I_c = J_c \times A_J$$
</Formula>

where $J_c$ is the critical current density (~100-500 A/cm²).

<Tip>
The oxidation step is critical: more oxidation creates thicker barrier, lower $I_c$, and lower $E_J$. This provides tunability but requires precise process control.
</Tip>

### Typical Parameters

- Evaporation angles: ±15° to ±25°
- Al thickness: 20-50 nm per layer
- Oxidation: 100-1000 mbar × minutes (pressure × time)
- Junction size: 100-300 nm × 100-300 nm

## 4. Full Fabrication Process Flow

### Step 1: Substrate Preparation

1. Clean silicon wafer (piranha, HF dip)
2. Optional: Deposit TiN ground plane
3. Spin resist for first lithography layer

### Step 2: Large Features (Optical Lithography)

1. Pattern capacitor pads, resonators, wiring
2. Metal deposition (Al or Nb)
3. Liftoff or etching

### Step 3: Junction Definition (E-beam Lithography)

1. Spin bilayer resist
2. E-beam write junction patterns
3. Develop with precise timing
4. Check undercut with SEM

### Step 4: Junction Evaporation

1. Load into evaporator
2. First Al evaporation (e.g., +20°, 30 nm)
3. Static oxidation (e.g., 200 mbar, 10 min)
4. Second Al evaporation (e.g., -20°, 50 nm)
5. Liftoff in warm acetone

### Step 5: Post-Processing

1. Dice wafer into chips
2. Wire bond to sample holder
3. Room temperature testing
4. Cool down and characterize

<Example>
Estimating junction parameters from fabrication:

```python
import numpy as np

def estimate_junction_params(width_nm, length_nm, J_c_A_cm2, oxidation_factor=1.0):
    """
    Estimate Josephson junction parameters from geometry.

    Args:
        width_nm: Junction width in nm
        length_nm: Junction length in nm
        J_c_A_cm2: Critical current density in A/cm^2
        oxidation_factor: Modifier for oxidation (>1 = more oxidation = lower Jc)

    Returns:
        Dictionary with junction parameters
    """
    # Convert to cm^2
    area_cm2 = (width_nm * 1e-7) * (length_nm * 1e-7)

    # Critical current
    I_c = J_c_A_cm2 * area_cm2 / oxidation_factor  # Amps

    # Josephson energy E_J = h_bar * I_c / (2e)
    h_bar = 1.055e-34
    e = 1.6e-19
    E_J_J = h_bar * I_c / (2 * e)
    E_J_GHz = E_J_J / (6.626e-34 * 1e9)

    # Junction capacitance (typical ~50 fF/um^2)
    C_J_fF = 50 * (width_nm/1000) * (length_nm/1000)

    return {
        'area_um2': (width_nm/1000) * (length_nm/1000),
        'I_c_nA': I_c * 1e9,
        'E_J_GHz': E_J_GHz,
        'C_J_fF': C_J_fF
    }

# Example: 200 nm x 200 nm junction, J_c = 200 A/cm^2
params = estimate_junction_params(200, 200, 200)
for k, v in params.items():
    print(f"{k}: {v:.2f}")
```
</Example>

## 5. Materials and Coherence

### Two-Level Systems (TLS)

**Two-level systems** are defects in materials that couple to qubits and cause decoherence. They arise from:

- **Surface oxides**: SiO₂ on Si, native oxides
- **Substrate interfaces**: Buried defects
- **Metal-substrate interfaces**: Disorder, contamination
- **Junction barrier**: AlOx is amorphous

### TLS Mitigation Strategies

1. **Surface treatments**: HF dip removes native oxide
2. **Substrate choice**: Sapphire has lower TLS density
3. **Encapsulation**: Protect surfaces from oxidation
4. **3D architectures**: Reduced surface participation

### Participation Ratio

The **participation ratio** $p_i$ describes how much of the electric field is in lossy region $i$:

<Formula>
$$\frac{1}{Q} = \sum_i p_i \tan\delta_i$$
</Formula>

where $\tan\delta_i$ is the loss tangent of region $i$.

<Warning>
Even a thin surface oxide with high loss tangent can dominate decoherence if the electric field is concentrated there. Modern qubit designs minimize field at surfaces.
</Warning>

## 6. Process Variations and Yield

### Sources of Variation

1. **Resist thickness**: Affects undercut and bridge
2. **E-beam dose**: Controls linewidth
3. **Development time**: Critical for undercut
4. **Oxidation conditions**: Determines $I_c$
5. **Film stress**: Can affect geometry

### Frequency Targeting

Junction parameters have uncertainty:
- $I_c$ variation: typically 5-15%
- This causes qubit frequency variation of similar magnitude

For a transmon with $f_{01} = \sqrt{8 E_J E_C} - E_C$:

<Formula>
$$\frac{\delta f}{f} \approx \frac{1}{2}\frac{\delta E_J}{E_J} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{\delta I_c}{I_c}$$
</Formula>

### Laser Annealing

Post-fabrication **laser annealing** can tune junction resistance:
- Heats junction locally
- Modifies barrier thickness/quality
- Can increase or decrease $I_c$
- Used to "trim" qubit frequencies

## 7. Scaling Considerations

### Challenges at Scale

- **Yield**: Each junction must work
- **Uniformity**: Frequencies must be close (for coupling)
- **Wiring density**: More qubits = more connections
- **Crosstalk**: Fabrication affects electromagnetic isolation

### Industrial Approaches

Modern quantum computing companies use:
- 300 mm wafer processing
- Optical lithography where possible
- Advanced process control
- Statistical process monitoring

## 8. Summary

Superconducting qubit fabrication is a precise art:

1. **Substrates**: Silicon or sapphire, carefully prepared
2. **Dolan bridge**: Shadow evaporation creates junctions
3. **Oxidation**: Controls junction properties
4. **Materials quality**: TLS in surfaces limit coherence
5. **Process control**: Variations affect yield and frequency
6. **Scaling**: Industrial processes enable large arrays

Understanding fabrication helps connect qubit design to measured performance and guides improvement efforts.
