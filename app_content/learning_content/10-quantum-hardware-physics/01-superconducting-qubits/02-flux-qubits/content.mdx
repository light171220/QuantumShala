## 1. Introduction to Flux Qubits

While transmon qubits encode information in charge states, **flux qubits** encode quantum information in the direction of circulating supercurrent around a loop containing Josephson junctions.

<Definition term="Flux Qubit">
A flux qubit is a superconducting qubit where the two computational states correspond to persistent currents circulating in opposite directions (clockwise and counterclockwise) around a superconducting loop containing one or more Josephson junctions.
</Definition>

The two basis states can be visualized as:
- $|0\rangle \equiv |\circlearrowleft\rangle$: Current flowing counterclockwise
- $|1\rangle \equiv |\circlearrowright\rangle$: Current flowing clockwise

These states carry magnetic flux in opposite directions, making flux qubits natural for coupling to magnetic fields and other flux-based systems.

## 2. The RF-SQUID Flux Qubit

### Basic Structure

The simplest flux qubit is the **RF-SQUID** (Radio Frequency Superconducting Quantum Interference Device), consisting of a superconducting loop interrupted by a single Josephson junction.

### The Flux Quantization Constraint

In a superconducting loop, the total flux must be quantized. The phase across the junction is related to the flux through:

<Formula>
$$\phi = 2\pi \frac{\Phi}{\Phi_0}$$
</Formula>

where $\Phi$ is the total flux through the loop (external plus self-induced) and $\Phi_0 = h/(2e)$ is the flux quantum.

### Potential Energy

The potential energy of the RF-SQUID as a function of the flux $\Phi$ is:

<Formula>
$$U(\Phi) = \frac{(\Phi - \Phi_{ext})^2}{2L} - E_J\cos\left(2\pi\frac{\Phi}{\Phi_0}\right)$$
</Formula>

where:
- $L$ is the loop inductance
- $\Phi_{ext}$ is the externally applied flux
- $E_J$ is the Josephson energy

### Double-Well Potential

When operated near $\Phi_{ext} = \Phi_0/2$ (the "frustration" or "degeneracy" point), the potential becomes a double-well:

<Formula>
$$U(\Phi) \approx U_0 + \frac{a}{2}(\Phi - \Phi_0/2)^2 - \frac{b}{4}(\Phi - \Phi_0/2)^4$$
</Formula>

The two wells correspond to the two circulation directions of the persistent current.

<Tip>
At $\Phi_{ext} = \Phi_0/2$, the two wells have equal depth, and quantum tunneling between them creates symmetric and antisymmetric superposition states - the qubit eigenstates.
</Tip>

## 3. Three-Junction Flux Qubit

### Why Three Junctions?

The single-junction RF-SQUID requires very large loop inductance to create a double-well potential, making it physically large and sensitive to flux noise. The **three-junction flux qubit** (also called the persistent current qubit) solves this problem.

### Circuit Design

The three-junction flux qubit consists of:
- A superconducting loop with three Josephson junctions
- Two identical junctions with Josephson energy $E_J$
- One smaller junction with energy $\alpha E_J$ where $\alpha < 1$ (typically 0.5-0.8)

### Hamiltonian

The potential energy depends on two phase variables and can be written as:

<Formula>
$$U(\phi_p, \phi_m) = 2E_J\left[1 - \cos\phi_p\cos\phi_m\right] + \alpha E_J\left[1 - \cos(2\pi f + 2\phi_m)\right]$$
</Formula>

where:
- $\phi_p = (\phi_1 + \phi_2)/2$ is the symmetric phase combination
- $\phi_m = (\phi_1 - \phi_2)/2$ is the antisymmetric phase combination
- $f = \Phi_{ext}/\Phi_0$ is the frustration parameter

### Operating Point

At $f = 0.5$ (half flux quantum):
- The potential has a double-well structure in the $\phi_m$ direction
- The wells correspond to opposite circulation directions
- Tunneling creates the qubit states

<Example>
For a three-junction flux qubit with $E_J/h = 200$ GHz and $\alpha = 0.75$:

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Parameters
E_J = 200  # GHz
alpha = 0.75
f = 0.5  # At degeneracy point

# Calculate potential along the tunneling direction
phi_m = np.linspace(-np.pi, np.pi, 200)
phi_p = 0  # Minimized in this direction

# Potential energy (in units of E_J)
U = 2 * (1 - np.cos(phi_p) * np.cos(phi_m)) + alpha * (1 - np.cos(2*np.pi*f + 2*phi_m))
U_normalized = U / (2 + alpha)  # Normalize

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(phi_m / np.pi, U_normalized)
plt.xlabel('Phase φ_m / π')
plt.ylabel('Potential Energy (normalized)')
plt.title('Double-Well Potential of Three-Junction Flux Qubit at f=0.5')
plt.axhline(y=0, color='k', linestyle='--', alpha=0.3)
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
```
</Example>

## 4. Persistent Current and Energy Scales

### Persistent Current

In the two potential wells, the qubit carries a persistent current:

<Formula>
$$I_p = I_c \sqrt{1 - (1/2\alpha)^2}$$
</Formula>

For typical parameters, $I_p \sim 300$ nA, corresponding to about $10^6$ Cooper pairs circulating.

### Energy Gap at Degeneracy

At the degeneracy point ($f = 0.5$), the qubit frequency is determined by the tunneling rate:

<Formula>
$$\Delta = \hbar\omega_{01} = 2t$$
</Formula>

where $t$ is the tunneling matrix element through the barrier.

### Away from Degeneracy

When $f \neq 0.5$, the wells have different depths, creating an energy bias:

<Formula>
$$\epsilon = 2I_p\Phi_0(f - 0.5)$$
</Formula>

The qubit frequency becomes:

<Formula>
$$\hbar\omega_{01} = \sqrt{\Delta^2 + \epsilon^2}$$
</Formula>

<Warning>
The flux qubit frequency depends strongly on the external flux bias. First-order sensitivity to flux noise occurs everywhere except exactly at $f = 0.5$ (the "sweet spot"), making flux qubits more challenging to operate than transmons.
</Warning>

## 5. The Fluxonium: Modern Flux Qubit

### Motivation

The **fluxonium** qubit addresses limitations of traditional flux qubits by replacing the large geometric inductance with a Josephson junction array.

<Definition term="Fluxonium">
The fluxonium is a flux qubit variant where a small Josephson junction is shunted by a large "superinductance" made from an array of larger Josephson junctions. This creates a qubit with both large anharmonicity and protection from charge noise.
</Definition>

### Circuit Elements

1. **Small junction**: Provides the nonlinearity (Josephson energy $E_J$)
2. **Superinductance**: Array of $N$ large junctions (total inductance $L = N\Phi_0/(2\pi I_{c,array})$)
3. **Shunt capacitance**: Controls the charging energy $E_C$

### Hamiltonian

<Formula>
$$\hat{H} = 4E_C\hat{n}^2 + \frac{1}{2}E_L\hat{\phi}^2 - E_J\cos(\hat{\phi} - 2\pi\Phi_{ext}/\Phi_0)$$
</Formula>

where $E_L = (\Phi_0/2\pi)^2/L$ is the inductive energy.

### Key Parameters

The fluxonium operates in the regime:
- $E_J/E_C \gg 1$ (like transmon, for charge noise immunity)
- $E_L \ll E_J$ (heavy fluxonium regime)
- $E_C \ll E_J$ (but $E_C$ can be larger than in transmons)

### Advantages of Fluxonium

| Property | Transmon | Traditional Flux Qubit | Fluxonium |
|----------|----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Anharmonicity | ~200 MHz | ~1-5 GHz | ~1-5 GHz |
| Charge noise | Immune | Immune | Immune |
| Flux noise | N/A | Sensitive | Protected at sweet spots |
| T1 potential | 100s μs | 10s μs | ms possible |

<Tip>
The fluxonium has demonstrated some of the longest coherence times among superconducting qubits, with T1 exceeding 1 ms in some experiments. Its large anharmonicity also enables faster gates.
</Tip>

## 6. Flux Qubit Readout and Control

### Readout Methods

1. **DC-SQUID magnetometer**: Measures the flux generated by the persistent current
2. **Dispersive readout**: Couples to a resonator (like transmon)
3. **Josephson bifurcation amplifier**: High-sensitivity flux detection

### Control

- **Single-qubit gates**: Microwave pulses at the qubit frequency
- **Flux pulses**: Rapid changes in $\Phi_{ext}$ for Z-rotations
- **Two-qubit gates**: Direct inductive coupling or tunable coupler

### Flux Noise and Sweet Spots

Flux noise is the primary decoherence source. At the sweet spot ($f = 0.5$):

<Formula>
$$\frac{\partial\omega_{01}}{\partial\Phi_{ext}} = 0$$
</Formula>

providing first-order protection against flux fluctuations.

## 7. Comparison: Flux Qubits vs Transmons

| Aspect | Flux Qubit | Transmon |
|--------|------------|----------|
| Qubit states | Persistent currents | Charge states |
| Typical frequency | 1-10 GHz | 4-6 GHz |
| Anharmonicity | Large (~GHz) | Small (~200 MHz) |
| Primary noise | Flux noise | Charge noise (suppressed) |
| Sweet spot | Flux degeneracy | Charge degeneracy (always there) |
| Fabrication | More complex | Simpler |
| Scalability | Challenging | Demonstrated at scale |

## 8. Summary

Flux qubits offer an alternative approach to superconducting quantum computing:

1. **Information encoding**: Persistent current direction (clockwise/counterclockwise)
2. **Double-well potential**: Created by flux frustration near $\Phi_0/2$
3. **Three-junction design**: Enables compact flux qubits with practical parameters
4. **Fluxonium**: Modern evolution with superinductance, offering large anharmonicity and potential for very long coherence
5. **Trade-offs**: Large anharmonicity but flux noise sensitivity (mitigated at sweet spots)

While transmons dominate current large-scale processors, flux qubits and especially fluxonium remain active areas of research due to their potential for longer coherence times and faster gates.
