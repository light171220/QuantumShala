## 1. Introduction: Why Coherence Matters

Quantum computation requires maintaining quantum superposition states long enough to complete algorithms. **Coherence time** measures how long a qubit retains its quantum properties before environmental interactions destroy the quantum information.

<Definition term="Coherence Time">
Coherence time is the characteristic timescale over which a qubit loses its quantum properties due to interactions with its environment. It sets the fundamental limit on how many quantum operations can be performed.
</Definition>

For practical quantum computing:
- Gate time: ~20-50 ns (for superconducting qubits)
- Error correction threshold: ~0.1-1% error per gate
- Required coherence: T >> gate time, ideally T > 1000 × gate time

## 2. The Bloch Sphere Picture

A qubit state can be visualized on the Bloch sphere:

<Formula>
$$|\psi\rangle = \cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle$$
</Formula>

The state is characterized by:
- **Polar angle** $\theta$: determines the probability of measuring |0⟩ vs |1⟩
- **Azimuthal angle** $\phi$: determines the relative phase

Decoherence affects these differently:
- **T1 processes**: Change $\theta$ (energy decay toward |0⟩)
- **T2 processes**: Randomize $\phi$ (phase loss)

## 3. T1: Energy Relaxation Time

### Physical Meaning

**T1** (also called longitudinal relaxation time) measures how long an excited qubit takes to decay to its ground state.

<Definition term="T1 (Energy Relaxation)">
T1 is the characteristic time for an excited qubit to relax to the ground state due to energy exchange with the environment. After time T1, the excited state population decays to 1/e of its initial value.
</Definition>

The excited state probability decays exponentially:

<Formula>
$$P_1(t) = P_1(0) \cdot e^{-t/T_1}$$
</Formula>

### Relaxation Mechanisms

Common T1 mechanisms in superconducting qubits:

1. **Purcell effect**: Decay through the readout resonator
   <Formula>
   $$\Gamma_P = \frac{g^2\kappa}{\Delta^2}$$
   </Formula>
   where $g$ is qubit-resonator coupling, $\kappa$ is resonator linewidth, $\Delta$ is detuning

2. **Dielectric loss**: Energy absorbed by two-level systems (TLS) in materials
   <Formula>
   $$\Gamma_{TLS} = \omega \tan\delta \cdot p_{TLS}$$
   </Formula>
   where $\tan\delta$ is the loss tangent and $p_{TLS}$ is participation ratio

3. **Quasiparticle tunneling**: Non-equilibrium quasiparticles cause transitions
   <Formula>
   $$\Gamma_{qp} \propto n_{qp} \cdot \sqrt{\omega/\Delta_{gap}}$$
   </Formula>

4. **Radiation loss**: Coupling to electromagnetic modes

<Example>
For a transmon with qubit-resonator detuning Δ/2π = 1 GHz, coupling g/2π = 100 MHz, and resonator linewidth κ/2π = 1 MHz:

```python
import numpy as np

# Purcell decay rate
g = 100e6  # Hz
kappa = 1e6  # Hz
Delta = 1e9  # Hz

Gamma_P = g**2 * kappa / Delta**2
T1_Purcell = 1 / Gamma_P

print(f"Purcell-limited T1 = {T1_Purcell*1e6:.1f} microseconds")
```

Result: T1_Purcell ≈ 100 μs

This shows why we need sufficient qubit-resonator detuning!
</Example>

### Fermi's Golden Rule

The relaxation rate is given by Fermi's Golden Rule:

<Formula>
$$\Gamma_1 = \frac{1}{T_1} = \frac{|\langle 0|\hat{H}_{int}|1\rangle|^2}{\hbar^2} S_\xi(\omega_{01})$$
</Formula>

where $S_\xi(\omega)$ is the noise spectral density at the qubit frequency.

## 4. T2: Dephasing Time

### Physical Meaning

**T2** (transverse relaxation time) measures how long phase coherence is maintained.

<Definition term="T2 (Dephasing Time)">
T2 is the characteristic time for loss of phase coherence in a superposition state. It combines the effects of energy relaxation (T1) and pure dephasing processes.
</Definition>

The off-diagonal density matrix element decays as:

<Formula>
$$\rho_{01}(t) = \rho_{01}(0) \cdot e^{-t/T_2}$$
</Formula>

### Components of T2

T2 has two contributions:

<Formula>
$$\frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{2T_1} + \frac{1}{T_\phi}$$
</Formula>

where:
- $1/(2T_1)$: Dephasing from energy relaxation (each T1 event randomizes phase)
- $1/T_\phi$: **Pure dephasing** from frequency fluctuations

This gives the fundamental relationship:

<Formula>
$$T_2 \leq 2T_1$$
</Formula>

<Warning>
The limit T2 = 2T1 is reached when pure dephasing is negligible (T_phi → ∞). In practice, T2 is often much less than 2T1 due to low-frequency noise sources.
</Warning>

### Pure Dephasing Mechanisms

Pure dephasing arises from fluctuations in the qubit frequency:

1. **Charge noise**: Random charges near the qubit cause frequency shifts
2. **Flux noise**: Magnetic field fluctuations (for tunable qubits)
3. **Photon shot noise**: Thermal or residual photons in the resonator
4. **TLS fluctuators**: Two-level systems switching between states

<Formula>
$$\frac{1}{T_\phi} = \frac{1}{2}\left|\frac{\partial\omega}{\partial\lambda}\right|^2 S_\lambda(0)$$
</Formula>

where $\lambda$ is the fluctuating parameter and $S_\lambda(0)$ is its low-frequency noise.

## 5. T2*: Inhomogeneous Dephasing

### Free Induction Decay

**T2*** (T2-star) is measured in a Ramsey experiment without echo pulses. It includes both intrinsic T2 and slow drifts:

<Formula>
$$\frac{1}{T_2^*} = \frac{1}{T_2} + \frac{1}{T_{drift}}$$
</Formula>

### Echo Sequences

**Spin echo** (Hahn echo) refocuses slow drifts, measuring true T2:

1. Apply π/2 pulse (create superposition)
2. Wait time t/2
3. Apply π pulse (refocus)
4. Wait time t/2
5. Apply π/2 pulse (measure)

The echo refocuses phase accumulated from static frequency offsets, recovering T2 from T2*.

<Tip>
T2* is what you measure in a simple Ramsey experiment. T2 (or T2-echo) is measured using spin echo sequences. For superconducting qubits, typically T2* < T2 ≤ 2T1.
</Tip>

### CPMG Sequences

The Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) sequence uses multiple π pulses to filter out noise at different frequencies:

<Formula>
$$\text{CPMG-}N: \quad \frac{\pi}{2} - \left[\frac{\tau}{2N} - \pi - \frac{\tau}{2N}\right]^N - \frac{\pi}{2}$$
</Formula>

More pulses = better filtering of higher frequency noise.

## 6. Noise Spectral Density

### 1/f Noise

Many systems exhibit **1/f noise** (pink noise):

<Formula>
$$S(\omega) = \frac{A^2}{|\omega|^\alpha}$$
</Formula>

with $\alpha$ typically near 1.

For 1/f charge noise in superconducting qubits:
- Amplitude: $\sqrt{S_q} \sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-4}$ $e/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$

For 1/f flux noise:
- Amplitude: $\sqrt{S_\Phi} \sim 1$ to $10$ $\mu\Phi_0/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$

### Calculating T_phi from Noise Spectra

For a qubit with frequency sensitivity $\partial\omega/\partial\lambda$:

<Formula>
$$\frac{1}{T_\phi} = \int_0^\infty d\omega \left|\frac{\partial\omega}{\partial\lambda}\right|^2 S_\lambda(\omega) F(\omega t)$$
</Formula>

where $F(\omega t)$ is the filter function determined by the pulse sequence.

<Example>
For Ramsey with free evolution time t:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import integrate

def calculate_T_phi_ramsey(d_omega_d_lambda, A_noise, alpha=1):
    """
    Calculate T_phi for 1/f^alpha noise in a Ramsey experiment.

    For 1/f noise (alpha=1): T_phi ~ 1 / (d_omega_d_lambda * A * sqrt(ln(t/t_min)))
    """
    # Simplified formula for 1/f noise
    # T_phi ~ 1 / (sensitivity^2 * A^2 * ln_factor)
    ln_factor = 10  # typical ln(bandwidth ratio)

    T_phi = 1 / (d_omega_d_lambda**2 * A_noise**2 * ln_factor)**0.5
    return T_phi

# Example: Flux noise
d_omega_d_Phi = 100e6  # 100 MHz per Phi_0 - typical transmon at flux sweet spot
A_flux = 1e-6  # 1 micro-Phi_0 / sqrt(Hz)

T_phi = calculate_T_phi_ramsey(d_omega_d_Phi, A_flux)
print(f"Estimated T_phi from flux noise: {T_phi*1e6:.1f} microseconds")
```
</Example>

## 7. Measuring Coherence Times

### T1 Measurement Protocol

1. Prepare qubit in |1⟩ (π pulse)
2. Wait variable time t
3. Measure
4. Fit exponential decay: $P_1(t) = A \cdot e^{-t/T_1} + B$

### T2* (Ramsey) Measurement

1. Apply π/2 pulse
2. Wait time t (qubit precesses at detuning frequency)
3. Apply π/2 pulse
4. Measure
5. Fit decaying oscillation: $P_1(t) = A \cdot e^{-t/T_2^*} \cos(\Delta\omega t + \phi) + B$

### T2 (Echo) Measurement

1. Apply π/2 pulse
2. Wait time t/2
3. Apply π pulse (echo)
4. Wait time t/2
5. Apply π/2 pulse
6. Measure
7. Fit exponential: $P_1(t) = A \cdot e^{-t/T_2} + B$

## 8. Coherence in Modern Qubits

### State of the Art (2024)

| Qubit Type | Typical T1 | Typical T2 | Record T1 |
|------------|------------|------------|-----------|
| Transmon | 50-200 μs | 50-150 μs | >500 μs |
| Fluxonium | 100-500 μs | 50-200 μs | >1 ms |
| 3D transmon | 100-300 μs | 100-200 μs | >1 ms |

### Strategies for Improved Coherence

1. **Materials**: High-purity substrates, improved fabrication, TLS reduction
2. **Design**: 3D cavities, Purcell filters, optimized geometries
3. **Operation**: Flux sweet spots, charge sweet spots, dynamical decoupling
4. **Environment**: Better shielding, lower temperatures, fewer quasiparticles

## 9. Summary

Understanding coherence is essential for quantum computing:

1. **T1 (relaxation)**: Energy decay from |1⟩ to |0⟩, limited by material losses and coupling to environment
2. **T2 (dephasing)**: Phase coherence loss, bounded by 2T1, often limited by low-frequency noise
3. **T2* (free decay)**: Includes slow drifts, measured in simple Ramsey
4. **Fundamental relation**: T2 ≤ 2T1 always holds
5. **Noise spectroscopy**: Understanding noise spectra guides qubit improvement

Modern transmons routinely achieve T1, T2 > 100 μs, enabling quantum error correction with surface codes.
