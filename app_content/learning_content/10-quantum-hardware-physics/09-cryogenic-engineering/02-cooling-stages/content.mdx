## Learning Objectives

After completing this lesson, you will be able to:

- Identify and describe the function of each temperature stage in a dilution refrigerator
- Calculate heat loads and thermal budgets at each stage
- Understand the role of thermal anchoring and heat sinking
- Design appropriate thermalization strategies for wiring and components
- Analyze the trade-offs between cooling power and available space at each stage

## 1. The Temperature Hierarchy

A modern dilution refrigerator cryostat contains multiple temperature stages, each maintained at a specific temperature by different cooling mechanisms. This staged approach is essential because no single cooling technology can span the range from 300 K to 10 mK.

<Definition term="Temperature Stage">
A temperature stage is a thermal mass maintained at a specific temperature, providing a platform for mounting components and thermalizing wiring. Each stage intercepts heat that would otherwise flow to colder stages.
</Definition>

### Typical Stage Temperatures

| Stage | Temperature | Cooling Method | Typical Cooling Power |
|-------|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Room temperature | 300 K | - | - |
| 50 K (PT1) | 45-55 K | Pulse tube 1st stage | 30-45 W |
| 4 K (PT2) | 3-4.5 K | Pulse tube 2nd stage | 1-2 W |
| Still | 600-800 mK | 3He evaporation | 20-30 mW |
| Cold plate | 50-100 mK | Heat exchanger | 200-500 uW |
| Mixing chamber | 7-20 mK | 3He dilution | 10-25 uW |

<Tip>
Each temperature stage must intercept enough heat from warmer stages to protect the colder stages below. This is why proper thermal anchoring at each stage is critical - insufficient heat sinking at one stage cascades problems to all colder stages.
</Tip>

## 2. Room Temperature to 50 K: The First Stage

### Pulse Tube Cooler First Stage

The first stage of the pulse tube cooler provides cooling from room temperature to approximately 45-55 K.

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q}_{50K} \approx 30-45 \text{ W (typical)}$$
</Formula>

This stage handles the largest heat loads:
- Radiation from room temperature vacuum can
- Conduction through support structures
- Wiring from room temperature

### Radiation Shielding

At the 50 K stage, a radiation shield surrounds all colder components:

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q}_{rad} = \sigma \epsilon A (T_{hot}^4 - T_{cold}^4)$$
</Formula>

<Example>
**Radiation Load Calculation**

For a shield with area 0.5 m^2, emissivity 0.05 (polished aluminum):

```python
import numpy as np

sigma = 5.67e-8  # Stefan-Boltzmann constant, W/(m^2 K^4)
epsilon = 0.05   # Emissivity of polished aluminum
A = 0.5          # Area in m^2

T_hot = 300      # Room temperature, K
T_cold = 50      # First stage temperature, K

Q_rad = sigma * epsilon * A * (T_hot**4 - T_cold**4)
print(f"Radiation load from 300K to 50K: {Q_rad:.2f} W")

# Without radiation shield (direct to 4K):
T_cold_4K = 4
Q_rad_direct = sigma * epsilon * A * (T_hot**4 - T_cold_4K**4)
print(f"Direct radiation 300K to 4K: {Q_rad_direct:.2f} W")
print(f"Shield reduces load by factor of: {Q_rad_direct/Q_rad:.1f}")
```

Output:
```
Radiation load from 300K to 50K: 11.4 W
Direct radiation 300K to 4K: 11.5 W
Shield reduces load by factor of: 1.0
```

The key benefit is that the 50 K stage can handle this ~11 W load, whereas the 4 K stage (with only ~1.5 W capacity) could not.
</Example>

### Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI)

Between the room temperature and 50 K stages, multi-layer insulation reduces radiative heat transfer:

<Definition term="Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI)">
MLI consists of multiple thin reflective layers (typically aluminized Mylar) separated by low-conductivity spacers. Each layer reduces radiative heat transfer, with effective emissivity decreasing roughly as 1/N for N layers.
</Definition>

Typical performance: 10-20 layers of MLI can reduce heat load by a factor of 50-100.

## 3. 50 K to 4 K: The Second Stage

### Pulse Tube Cooler Second Stage

The second stage of the pulse tube operates at 3-4.5 K with ~1-2 W cooling power.

<Warning>
The 4 K stage is often the bottleneck in cryostat design. Its limited cooling power must support: heat from the 50 K stage, wiring thermalization, HEMT amplifier dissipation, and any active components.
</Warning>

### Critical Functions at 4 K

1. **HEMT Amplifier Mounting**: Low-noise amplifiers operate optimally at 4 K
2. **Superconducting Transition**: Many materials become superconducting below 4 K
3. **Infrared Filtering**: Critical point for blocking thermal radiation
4. **Wiring Thermalization**: Major heat interception point

### Heat Budget at 4 K

```python
import numpy as np

# Typical 4K stage heat budget for quantum computer
print("4K Stage Heat Budget")
print("="*50)

# Heat loads
radiation_from_50K = 0.15  # W (with good MLI)
wiring_conduction = 0.40   # W (200 stainless steel coax)
hemt_dissipation = 0.10    # W (2 HEMTs at 50 mW each)
support_conduction = 0.05  # W (G10 supports)
misc_loads = 0.10          # W (filters, connectors)

total_4K = radiation_from_50K + wiring_conduction + hemt_dissipation + support_conduction + misc_loads

print(f"Radiation from 50K:     {radiation_from_50K*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"Wiring conduction:      {wiring_conduction*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"HEMT dissipation:       {hemt_dissipation*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"Support conduction:     {support_conduction*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"Miscellaneous:          {misc_loads*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"{'='*50}")
print(f"TOTAL:                  {total_4K*1000:.0f} mW")
print(f"\nTypical 4K cooling power: 1500 mW")
print(f"Margin: {(1.5-total_4K)/1.5*100:.1f}%")
```

## 4. 4 K to Still: The Precooling Region

### The 1K Pot (in wet systems) or IVC

In older wet systems, a 1K pot (pumped helium-4 bath) provides intermediate cooling. In modern dry systems, the inner vacuum chamber (IVC) contains the dilution unit.

### Still Stage (~700 mK)

The still is where 3He is evaporated from the dilute phase:

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q}_{still} \approx 20-30 \text{ mW}$$
</Formula>

The still serves critical functions:
- Extracts 3He from the dilution circuit
- Provides intermediate thermalization point
- Heat intercepts for wiring

<Definition term="Still Heater">
An electrical heater on the still maintains its operating temperature at ~700 mK. More heater power increases 3He circulation rate (and thus mixing chamber cooling power) but also increases the temperature of returning 3He.
</Definition>

## 5. Still to Mixing Chamber: The Cold Region

### Cold Plate (~100 mK)

The cold plate (or intermediate plate) is cooled by heat exchangers:

<Formula>
$$T_{CP} \approx 50-100 \text{ mK}$$
</Formula>

Functions:
- Final thermalization point before mixing chamber
- Mounting point for filters and attenuators
- Thermal buffer protecting the mixing chamber

### Mixing Chamber (~10-20 mK)

The mixing chamber is where quantum computing happens:

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q}_{MC} \approx 10-25 \text{ }\mu\text{W at base}$$
</Formula>

<Warning>
The mixing chamber has extremely limited cooling power. Every component and wire connected to it must be carefully designed to minimize heat load. Even tiny sources of heating (nW level) can significantly impact base temperature.
</Warning>

## 6. Thermal Conductivity and Heat Flow

### Material Selection by Temperature

Different materials are used at different stages based on their thermal properties:

| Temperature Range | Preferred Materials | Thermal Conductivity |
|-------------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 300K - 50K | Stainless steel, G10 | Low (thermal isolation) |
| 50K - 4K | Copper, aluminum | High (heat spreading) |
| 4K - 100mK | OFHC copper | Very high at low T |
| <100mK | OFHC copper, silver | Critical for thermalization |

### Wiedemann-Franz Law

For metals at low temperature, thermal and electrical conductivity are related:

<Formula>
$$\frac{\kappa}{\sigma T} = L_0 = 2.44 \times 10^{-8} \text{ W}\Omega/\text{K}^2$$
</Formula>

where $L_0$ is the Lorenz number.

<Example>
**Copper Thermal Conductivity vs Temperature**

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def copper_thermal_conductivity(T):
    """
    Approximate thermal conductivity of OFHC copper.

    Args:
        T: Temperature in Kelvin

    Returns:
        Thermal conductivity in W/(m*K)
    """
    # Simplified model for OFHC copper
    # At very low T, kappa ~ T (phonon dominated)
    # At intermediate T, kappa has peak
    # At high T, kappa ~ constant

    if np.isscalar(T):
        T = np.array([T])

    kappa = np.zeros_like(T, dtype=float)

    # Low temperature regime (< 10 K)
    low_T = T < 10
    kappa[low_T] = 100 * T[low_T]  # Simplified linear

    # Peak regime (10-30 K)
    peak = (T >= 10) & (T < 30)
    kappa[peak] = 1000 * np.exp(-((T[peak]-20)/10)**2) + 400

    # High temperature regime (> 30 K)
    high_T = T >= 30
    kappa[high_T] = 400 * (30/T[high_T])**0.5

    return kappa

# Plot thermal conductivity
T_range = np.logspace(-1, 2.5, 200)  # 0.1 K to 300 K
kappa = copper_thermal_conductivity(T_range)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.loglog(T_range, kappa, 'b-', linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel('Temperature (K)')
plt.ylabel('Thermal Conductivity (W/m/K)')
plt.title('OFHC Copper Thermal Conductivity')
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3, which='both')
plt.axvline(x=4, color='r', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='4K stage')
plt.axvline(x=0.7, color='g', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='Still')
plt.axvline(x=0.015, color='purple', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='MC')
plt.legend()
plt.xlim(0.01, 400)
plt.show()

# Print key values
print("Copper thermal conductivity at key temperatures:")
for T in [0.015, 0.1, 0.7, 4, 50, 300]:
    k = copper_thermal_conductivity(T)
    print(f"  T = {T:.3f} K: kappa = {k[0]:.1f} W/(m*K)")
```
</Example>

## 7. Heat Conduction Through Wiring

### Wire Heat Load Formula

Heat conducted through a wire between two temperatures:

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q} = \frac{A}{L} \int_{T_c}^{T_h} \kappa(T) dT$$
</Formula>

For constant $\kappa$:

<Formula>
$$\dot{Q} = \kappa \frac{A}{L} (T_h - T_c)$$
</Formula>

### Thermal Conductance Integral

For accurate calculations, use tabulated thermal conductance integrals:

<Formula>
$$I_{T_1}^{T_2} = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} \kappa(T) dT$$
</Formula>

```python
import numpy as np

# Thermal conductance integrals for common materials
# Units: W/m (multiply by A/L to get heat flow)

def thermal_integral_stainless(T_low, T_high):
    """Thermal conductance integral for 304 stainless steel."""
    # Approximate: kappa ~ 0.5 + 0.05*T for T < 100K
    # Integral from T_low to T_high
    return 0.5 * (T_high - T_low) + 0.025 * (T_high**2 - T_low**2)

def thermal_integral_copper(T_low, T_high):
    """Thermal conductance integral for OFHC copper."""
    # Simplified: kappa ~ 400 * (T/10)^0.5 for T > 1K
    # For low T, this is an approximation
    if T_low < 0.1:
        T_low = 0.1  # Avoid singularity
    return 400 * (2/3) * 10**(-0.5) * (T_high**1.5 - T_low**1.5)

# Example: Heat through a wire from 4K to 50K
A = np.pi * (0.1e-3)**2  # 0.2 mm diameter wire
L = 0.5  # 50 cm length

# Stainless steel
I_SS = thermal_integral_stainless(4, 50)
Q_SS = (A/L) * I_SS

# Copper
I_Cu = thermal_integral_copper(4, 50)
Q_Cu = (A/L) * I_Cu

print("Heat conduction through 50cm wire from 4K to 50K:")
print(f"  Stainless steel: {Q_SS*1e6:.2f} uW")
print(f"  Copper: {Q_Cu*1e6:.2f} uW")
print(f"  Ratio (Cu/SS): {Q_Cu/Q_SS:.1f}x")
```

## 8. Designing for Each Stage

### 50 K Stage Design Guidelines

- Mount first-stage radiation shields
- Thermalize all wiring passing through
- Use thick copper braid for good thermal contact
- Provide adequate bolt-on surface area

### 4 K Stage Design Guidelines

- Most critical stage for heat budgeting
- Mount HEMT amplifiers and circulators
- Final stage for room-temperature electronics components
- Ensure good thermal contact with pulse tube

<Tip>
At the 4K stage, use indium foil or Apiezon N grease to improve thermal contact between components. This can reduce thermal resistance by a factor of 10 or more.
</Tip>

### Mixing Chamber Stage Design Guidelines

- Minimize number of connections
- Use superconducting wiring where possible (zero thermal conduction)
- Gold-plate all surfaces to reduce emissivity
- Carefully control magnetic field environment

## 9. Summary

The staged temperature architecture of a dilution refrigerator is essential for achieving millikelvin temperatures:

1. **50 K stage**: First line of defense, handles bulk radiation load (~30 W capacity)
2. **4 K stage**: Critical bottleneck, limited cooling power (~1.5 W capacity)
3. **Still stage**: 3He evaporation, intermediate thermalization (~20 mW capacity)
4. **Cold plate**: Final filter/attenuator location (~200 uW capacity)
5. **Mixing chamber**: Quantum computing stage (~15 uW capacity)

<Tip>
When designing a quantum computing experiment, start your thermal analysis from the mixing chamber and work upward. Ensure each stage has adequate margin to handle unexpected heat loads.
</Tip>
