## 1. Introduction to Cryogenic Wiring

Connecting quantum devices at millikelvin temperatures to room-temperature electronics requires careful wiring design. Every wire is a potential pathway for:

- **Thermal noise** from warmer stages
- **Heat conduction** that increases base temperature
- **Electromagnetic interference** that degrades coherence
- **Infrared radiation** that causes qubit excitation

<Definition term="Cryogenic Wiring Challenge">
The fundamental challenge is transmitting signals with high fidelity while minimizing heat load and noise. This requires careful selection of materials, attenuation strategies, and filtering at multiple temperature stages.
</Definition>

### Types of Signal Lines

| Line Type | Frequency | Typical Use | Key Challenge |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| DC bias | 0-100 Hz | Flux tuning, gate voltages | Filtering noise |
| Baseband | 100 Hz - 1 GHz | Pulse shaping | Bandwidth vs noise |
| Microwave | 1-20 GHz | Qubit control, readout | Attenuation vs signal |
| Output | 4-8 GHz | Readout signal | Amplification at cold |

## 2. DC and Low-Frequency Lines

### Wire Material Selection

For DC and low-frequency signals, thermal conductivity is the primary concern:

| Material | Resistivity (uOhm-cm) | Thermal Cond. at 4K (W/m/K) | Use Case |
|----------|----------------------|----------------------------|----------|
| Copper | 1.7 | 400 | High current, short runs |
| Phosphor bronze | 10 | 20 | Twisted pairs |
| Manganin | 43 | 15 | Resistance thermometry |
| Constantan | 50 | 10 | Very low thermal load |
| NbTi (SC) | 0 below 10K | ~0.1 | Zero heat, limited current |

<Tip>
Superconducting NbTi wire has essentially zero thermal conductivity below its critical temperature (~10 K). Use it for connections between the 4K stage and mixing chamber to eliminate conducted heat.
</Tip>

### Filtering DC Lines

DC lines require aggressive filtering to remove:
1. Johnson noise from room-temperature resistors
2. High-frequency pickup
3. Digital switching noise

<Formula>
$$V_{noise} = \sqrt{4 k_B T R \Delta f}$$
</Formula>

<Example>
**DC Filter Design**

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def johnson_noise(T, R, bandwidth):
    """Calculate Johnson noise voltage."""
    k_B = 1.38e-23  # J/K
    return np.sqrt(4 * k_B * T * R * bandwidth)

def filter_attenuation(f, f_cutoff, order):
    """Calculate Butterworth filter attenuation."""
    return 1 / np.sqrt(1 + (f / f_cutoff)**(2 * order))

# Compare noise at different temperatures
T_values = [300, 50, 4, 0.7, 0.015]  # K
R = 1000  # 1 kOhm source
bw = 1e9  # 1 GHz bandwidth

print("Johnson noise at different temperatures (1 kOhm, 1 GHz BW):")
for T in T_values:
    V_n = johnson_noise(T, R, bw)
    print(f"  T = {T:6.3f} K: V_noise = {V_n*1e9:.2f} nV")

# Filter design for DC line
print("\nRC Filter Design:")
R_filter = 10e3  # 10 kOhm
C_filter = 1e-9  # 1 nF
f_c = 1 / (2 * np.pi * R_filter * C_filter)
print(f"  R = {R_filter/1e3:.0f} kOhm, C = {C_filter*1e9:.0f} nF")
print(f"  Cutoff frequency: {f_c:.0f} Hz")

# Multi-stage filtering
f_range = np.logspace(0, 9, 1000)  # 1 Hz to 1 GHz
attn_1stage = filter_attenuation(f_range, f_c, 1)
attn_2stage = filter_attenuation(f_range, f_c, 2)
attn_3stage = filter_attenuation(f_range, f_c, 3)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.semilogx(f_range, 20*np.log10(attn_1stage), label='1 stage')
plt.semilogx(f_range, 20*np.log10(attn_2stage), label='2 stages')
plt.semilogx(f_range, 20*np.log10(attn_3stage), label='3 stages')
plt.xlabel('Frequency (Hz)')
plt.ylabel('Attenuation (dB)')
plt.title('Multi-stage RC Filter Response')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.ylim(-100, 5)
plt.show()
```
</Example>

### Twisted Pair Wiring

Twisted pairs reduce pickup of external magnetic fields:

<Formula>
$$V_{pickup} \propto A \cdot \frac{dB}{dt}$$
</Formula>

where A is the loop area enclosed by the wire pair. Twisting reduces effective loop area.

## 3. Microwave (RF) Lines

### Coaxial Cable Types

Microwave signals require coaxial cables for impedance-controlled transmission:

| Cable Type | Material | Attn at 10 GHz | Thermal Load | Typical Use |
|------------|----------|----------------|--------------|-------------|
| UT-085-SS | Stainless steel | 5 dB/m | Low | 300K to 4K |
| UT-085-CuNi | Cupronickel | 4 dB/m | Very low | 4K to still |
| SC-086 | NbTi superconducting | 0.1 dB/m | Minimal | Still to MC |
| UT-034-SS | SS, small diameter | 15 dB/m | Very low | Dense routing |

<Warning>
Stainless steel coax provides good thermal isolation but has significant signal attenuation. For readout lines where signal preservation is critical, use superconducting cables at cold stages.
</Warning>

### Attenuation Strategy

Attenuators serve two purposes:
1. **Reduce thermal noise** from warmer stages
2. **Define the noise temperature** seen by the qubit

<Definition term="Effective Noise Temperature">
The noise power delivered to a device equals the sum of attenuated noise from each stage. The effective noise temperature is $T_{eff} = \sum_i T_i \cdot A_i$ where $A_i$ is the attenuation factor from stage i.
</Definition>

<Example>
**Attenuation Design**

```python
import numpy as np

def effective_noise_temp(stage_temps, attenuations_dB):
    """
    Calculate effective noise temperature seen by qubit.

    Args:
        stage_temps: Temperature of each stage (K)
        attenuations_dB: Attenuation at each stage (dB, cumulative from RT)

    Returns:
        Effective noise temperature in K
    """
    T_eff = 0
    for i, (T, A_dB) in enumerate(zip(stage_temps, attenuations_dB)):
        # Convert dB to linear attenuation factor
        if i == 0:
            A_prev = 1
        else:
            A_prev = 10**(-attenuations_dB[i-1]/10)
        A_curr = 10**(-A_dB/10)

        # This stage contributes T * (A_prev - A_curr)
        # Simplified: each stage's contribution after being attenuated
        contribution = T * (A_prev - A_curr)
        T_eff += max(0, contribution)

    return T_eff

# Standard attenuation scheme
stages = ['RT', '50K', '4K', 'Still', 'CP', 'MC']
temps = [300, 50, 4, 0.7, 0.1, 0.015]

# Different attenuation strategies
strategies = {
    'Minimal': [0, 0, 10, 10, 10, 10],
    'Standard': [0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50],
    'Aggressive': [0, 20, 40, 50, 60, 70],
}

print("Effective Noise Temperature Analysis")
print("="*60)
for name, attens in strategies.items():
    T_eff = effective_noise_temp(temps, attens)
    print(f"\n{name} strategy:")
    for s, t, a in zip(stages, temps, attens):
        print(f"  {s:5s}: T={t:6.3f} K, Cumulative attn={a:2d} dB")
    print(f"  -> Effective noise temp: {T_eff*1000:.2f} mK")

# For good qubit operation, we want T_eff < T_qubit (few mK)
print("\n" + "="*60)
print("Target: T_eff < 20 mK for good qubit initialization")
```
</Example>

### Heat Dissipation in Attenuators

Attenuators absorb power, which becomes heat:

<Formula>
$$P_{dissipated} = P_{in} \cdot (1 - 10^{-A_{dB}/10})$$
</Formula>

<Example>
**Attenuator Heat Load**

```python
import numpy as np

def attenuator_heat(P_in_dBm, attenuation_dB):
    """
    Calculate heat dissipated in an attenuator.

    Args:
        P_in_dBm: Input power in dBm
        attenuation_dB: Attenuation in dB

    Returns:
        Heat dissipated in Watts
    """
    P_in_W = 10**((P_in_dBm - 30) / 10)  # Convert dBm to Watts
    fraction_absorbed = 1 - 10**(-attenuation_dB / 10)
    return P_in_W * fraction_absorbed

# Typical qubit drive: -30 dBm at chip
# Working backwards through attenuation chain
print("RF Line Heat Budget")
print("="*60)

P_chip_dBm = -30  # Power at chip
stages = ['MC', 'CP', 'Still', '4K', '50K', 'RT']
attens = [10, 10, 10, 20, 10, 0]  # Attenuation at each stage (going up)

P_current = P_chip_dBm
print(f"Target power at chip: {P_chip_dBm} dBm")
print("\nWorking backwards from chip to room temperature:")

for stage, atten in zip(stages, attens):
    P_before = P_current + atten  # Power before this attenuator
    heat = attenuator_heat(P_before, atten)
    print(f"  {stage:5s}: {atten:2d} dB attenuator")
    print(f"         Power in: {P_before:6.1f} dBm, Heat: {heat*1e9:.2f} nW")
    P_current = P_before

print(f"\nRequired output from RT electronics: {P_current:.1f} dBm")
print(f"  = {10**((P_current-30)/10)*1000:.2f} mW")
```
</Example>

## 4. Infrared Filtering

### The IR Radiation Problem

Thermal infrared radiation from room temperature can:
1. Cause qubit excitation (photon energy > qubit energy)
2. Generate quasiparticles in superconductors
3. Heat the mixing chamber stage

<Formula>
$$E_{photon} = h\nu = hc/\lambda$$
</Formula>

At 300K, peak radiation is at ~10 um wavelength, corresponding to ~30 THz (~120 meV).

<Warning>
A typical transmon qubit has energy ~20 ueV (5 GHz). Room temperature IR photons carry 6000x more energy - even a tiny amount of IR leakage can be catastrophic for qubit coherence.
</Warning>

### IR Blocking Strategies

1. **Eccosorb filters**: Microwave-absorbing foam (blocks IR, passes microwave)
2. **Metal powder filters**: Lossy transmission line (broadband IR absorption)
3. **Blocking capacitors**: DC break with IR absorption
4. **Cryogenic circulators**: Provide isolation and some IR blocking

### Filter Placement

<Definition term="Eccosorb Filter">
A filter made from magnetically-loaded silicone foam (Eccosorb CR or MF series) that absorbs electromagnetic radiation. Effective from ~1 GHz to >100 GHz, providing both IR blocking and microwave filtering.
</Definition>

```python
# IR filter placement strategy
print("IR Filter Placement Strategy")
print("="*50)

stages = ['4K', 'Still', 'Cold Plate', 'MC']
filter_types = {
    '4K': 'Eccosorb CR-110 (broadband absorber)',
    'Still': 'Metal powder filter (copper in Stycast)',
    'Cold Plate': 'Eccosorb MF-110 or copper powder',
    'MC': 'Final stage IR tight, minimal filtering'
}

for stage in stages:
    print(f"\n{stage} Stage:")
    print(f"  Recommended: {filter_types[stage]}")

print("\nKey principles:")
print("  1. Block IR at warmest possible stage (easier cooling)")
print("  2. Multiple stages for redundancy")
print("  3. Verify with qubit T1 measurements")
```

## 5. Output Lines and Amplification

### HEMT Amplifiers

Low-noise amplification near 4K is essential for readout:

<Definition term="HEMT Amplifier">
High Electron Mobility Transistor amplifier. Operates at 4K with noise temperature 2-4K and gain 35-40 dB. Enables single-shot qubit readout by amplifying weak microwave signals before they acquire noise from room-temperature electronics.
</Definition>

| Parameter | Typical Value |
|-----------|---------------|
| Noise temperature | 2-5 K |
| Gain | 35-40 dB |
| Bandwidth | 4-8 GHz |
| Power dissipation | 5-15 mW |
| Operating temperature | 4 K |

### Traveling Wave Parametric Amplifiers (TWPA)

For even lower noise, quantum-limited amplifiers operate at millikelvin:

| Parameter | TWPA | HEMT |
|-----------|------|------|
| Noise | Quantum-limited (~0.5 photon) | 2-5 K |
| Gain | 15-20 dB | 35-40 dB |
| Bandwidth | 2-4 GHz | 4-8 GHz |
| Temperature | 10-20 mK | 4 K |
| Power consumption | ~0 (pump power) | 5-15 mW |

## 6. Complete Wiring Architecture

### Example: 100-Qubit System

```python
import numpy as np

class WiringArchitecture:
    """Design wiring for a multi-qubit system."""

    def __init__(self, n_qubits):
        self.n_qubits = n_qubits

        # Line count estimates
        self.n_xy_lines = n_qubits  # Individual XY drive
        self.n_z_lines = n_qubits   # Individual flux bias
        self.n_readout_in = n_qubits // 8  # Multiplexed readout drive
        self.n_readout_out = n_qubits // 8  # Multiplexed readout output

    def print_line_count(self):
        print(f"Wiring for {self.n_qubits}-qubit system:")
        print(f"  XY drive lines: {self.n_xy_lines}")
        print(f"  Z (flux) lines: {self.n_z_lines}")
        print(f"  Readout input: {self.n_readout_in}")
        print(f"  Readout output: {self.n_readout_out}")
        print(f"  Total lines: {self.total_lines()}")

    def total_lines(self):
        return self.n_xy_lines + self.n_z_lines + self.n_readout_in + self.n_readout_out

    def heat_budget(self):
        """Estimate heat load at each stage."""
        # Heat per line (approximate)
        heat_per_rf_line = {
            '50K': 100e-6,   # 100 uW
            '4K': 10e-6,     # 10 uW (attenuators)
            'still': 0.1e-6, # 100 nW
            'MC': 10e-9      # 10 nW
        }

        heat_per_dc_line = {
            '50K': 50e-6,    # 50 uW
            '4K': 5e-6,      # 5 uW
            'still': 50e-9,  # 50 nW
            'MC': 5e-9       # 5 nW
        }

        n_rf = self.n_xy_lines + self.n_readout_in + self.n_readout_out
        n_dc = self.n_z_lines

        heat = {}
        for stage in ['50K', '4K', 'still', 'MC']:
            heat[stage] = (n_rf * heat_per_rf_line[stage] +
                          n_dc * heat_per_dc_line[stage])

        return heat

# Design for 100 qubits
arch = WiringArchitecture(100)
arch.print_line_count()

print("\nEstimated heat budget:")
heat = arch.heat_budget()
for stage, h in heat.items():
    if h > 1e-3:
        print(f"  {stage}: {h*1e3:.1f} mW")
    else:
        print(f"  {stage}: {h*1e6:.1f} uW")
```

## 7. Practical Considerations

### Cable Management

- **Strain relief**: Prevent cable damage during thermal cycling
- **Routing**: Avoid sharp bends (minimum bend radius = 5x diameter)
- **Labeling**: Essential for maintenance and troubleshooting
- **Spares**: Include 10-20% spare lines

### Connectors

| Temperature | Connector Type | Notes |
|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Room temp | SMA, K-connector | Standard |
| 50K - 4K | SMA | May need re-torque after cycling |
| < 4K | SMP, SMPM | Smaller, lower thermal mass |

### Testing and Qualification

1. **Room temperature**: Verify S-parameters and DC continuity
2. **During cooldown**: Monitor resistance vs temperature
3. **At base**: Measure noise floor, crosstalk, and filter response
4. **With qubits**: Characterize T1, T2 to verify filtering adequacy

## 8. Summary

Cryogenic wiring design requires balancing multiple constraints:

1. **Signal integrity**: Maintain bandwidth and minimize distortion
2. **Thermal isolation**: Minimize heat conduction to cold stages
3. **Noise filtering**: Block thermal noise and IR radiation
4. **Practical installation**: Manage hundreds of cables efficiently

<Tip>
The wiring design often determines the ultimate performance of a quantum computer. Invest time in careful analysis and testing of the wiring architecture before committing to a specific design.
</Tip>
