## 1. The QCCD Concept

Proposed by Kielpinski, Monroe, and Wineland (2002), QCCD addresses scaling by:
- Keeping interaction zones small (few ions)
- Physically moving ions between zones
- Enabling large total qubit counts

## 2. Key Components

### Segmented Trap
- Many DC electrode segments for precise control
- Allows creating and moving potential wells
- Surface traps enable complex geometries

### Zones
- **Gate zones**: 2-4 ions for high-fidelity operations
- **Storage zones**: Park unused ions
- **Loading zones**: Ion source and initial trapping
- **Measurement zones**: State detection

### Junctions
- X, Y, or T junctions for routing
- Allow ion chains to be split and reconfigured

## 3. Shuttling Operations

### Linear Transport
Move ions along the trap axis:
- Typical speed: 1-10 m/s
- Distance: mm scale
- Time: 10-100 microseconds
- Heating: <1 phonon per transport

### Junction Crossing
Navigate through intersections:
- More complex than linear transport
- Requires careful voltage control
- ~100 microseconds per crossing

### Ion Reordering
Swap ion positions:
- Split chains, rotate, recombine
- Enables arbitrary connectivity
- ~1 ms for full reorder

## 4. Current State of the Art

Quantinuum H-series:
- 32+ qubits demonstrated
- >99.5% two-qubit gate fidelity
- Full connectivity via shuttling
- Continuous improvement trajectory

## 5. Advantages and Challenges

### Advantages
- High gate fidelities in small zones
- Arbitrary connectivity through shuttling
- Modular and scalable design

### Challenges
- Shuttling overhead in circuit depth
- Complex control electronics
- Junction crossing fidelity

## Summary

QCCD represents the most mature approach to scaling trapped ions, combining high-fidelity gates with flexible connectivity through physical ion transport.
