## 1. Introduction to Microwave Qubit Control

Superconducting qubits operate at microwave frequencies (typically 4-8 GHz), matching the energy gap between the computational states. Controlling these qubits requires precisely shaped microwave pulses that coherently manipulate the quantum state.

<Definition term="Microwave Qubit Control">
Microwave qubit control refers to the use of time-varying electromagnetic fields at GHz frequencies to perform unitary operations on superconducting qubits. The microwave field couples to the qubit's electric dipole moment, enabling coherent state manipulation.
</Definition>

The fundamental principle is that an oscillating electric field at the qubit frequency drives transitions between the |0⟩ and |1⟩ states, with the rotation angle determined by the pulse amplitude and duration.

## 2. Qubit-Field Coupling

### The Charge Coupling Mechanism

For a transmon qubit, the microwave field couples to the qubit through the charge operator. The drive Hamiltonian is:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H}_{drive} = \hbar \Omega(t) \cos(\omega_d t + \phi) \cdot \hat{n}$$
</Formula>

where:
- $\Omega(t)$ is the time-dependent drive amplitude (Rabi frequency envelope)
- $\omega_d$ is the drive frequency
- $\phi$ is the phase of the drive
- $\hat{n}$ is the charge number operator

In the qubit subspace, the charge operator connects states |0⟩ and |1⟩:

<Formula>
$$\hat{n} \approx n_{01}(|0\rangle\langle 1| + |1\rangle\langle 0|) = n_{01}\hat{\sigma}_x$$
</Formula>

where $n_{01} = \langle 0|\hat{n}|1\rangle$ is the charge matrix element.

### Total Qubit Hamiltonian

The complete Hamiltonian for a driven qubit is:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H} = \frac{\hbar\omega_q}{2}\hat{\sigma}_z + \hbar\Omega(t)\cos(\omega_d t + \phi)\hat{\sigma}_x$$
</Formula>

where $\omega_q$ is the qubit frequency.

<Tip>
The coupling strength $\Omega(t)$ is proportional to the drive amplitude and the charge matrix element. For transmons, typical Rabi frequencies range from 1-100 MHz, enabling gate times of 10-100 ns.
</Tip>

## 3. The Rotating Frame

### Why Use a Rotating Frame?

The qubit oscillates at GHz frequencies, but we're interested in the slower dynamics of the quantum state evolution during a gate. The rotating frame transformation removes the fast oscillation, revealing the relevant physics.

<Definition term="Rotating Frame">
The rotating frame is a mathematical transformation that removes the free evolution of the qubit at its natural frequency, leaving only the dynamics induced by the drive field. It's analogous to viewing a carousel from a seat that rotates with it.
</Definition>

### Frame Transformation

We transform to a frame rotating at the drive frequency $\omega_d$:

<Formula>
$$\hat{U}_{rot} = e^{i\omega_d t \hat{\sigma}_z/2}$$
</Formula>

The transformed state is $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle = \hat{U}_{rot}|\psi\rangle$, and the effective Hamiltonian becomes:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H}_{rot} = \hat{U}_{rot}\hat{H}\hat{U}_{rot}^\dagger - i\hbar\hat{U}_{rot}\frac{\partial\hat{U}_{rot}^\dagger}{\partial t}$$
</Formula>

### Rotating Wave Approximation

After the transformation, we get terms oscillating at $2\omega_d$ (fast) and DC terms (slow). The **Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA)** neglects the fast terms:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H}_{RWA} = \frac{\hbar\Delta}{2}\hat{\sigma}_z + \frac{\hbar\Omega(t)}{2}(\cos\phi\,\hat{\sigma}_x + \sin\phi\,\hat{\sigma}_y)$$
</Formula>

where $\Delta = \omega_q - \omega_d$ is the **detuning** between qubit and drive frequencies.

<Warning>
The RWA is valid when $\Omega \ll \omega_q$. For very strong drives or fast pulses, counter-rotating terms become significant and must be included, leading to Bloch-Siegert shifts and other effects.
</Warning>

## 4. Rabi Oscillations

### Resonant Driving

When the drive is on resonance ($\Delta = 0$) with phase $\phi = 0$, the Hamiltonian simplifies to:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H}_{RWA} = \frac{\hbar\Omega(t)}{2}\hat{\sigma}_x$$
</Formula>

This generates rotations about the X-axis of the Bloch sphere.

### The Rabi Formula

Starting from |0⟩ and applying a resonant drive for time $t$, the probability of finding the qubit in |1⟩ is:

<Formula>
$$P_1(t) = \sin^2\left(\frac{\Omega t}{2}\right)$$
</Formula>

This oscillation between |0⟩ and |1⟩ is called a **Rabi oscillation**.

<Example>
For a constant drive with $\Omega/2\pi = 50$ MHz:

- **π/2 pulse** (|0⟩ → |+⟩): $t_{\pi/2} = \pi/(2\Omega) = 5$ ns
- **π pulse** (|0⟩ → |1⟩): $t_\pi = \pi/\Omega = 10$ ns
- **2π pulse** (|0⟩ → |0⟩): $t_{2\pi} = 2\pi/\Omega = 20$ ns

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Rabi oscillation simulation
Omega = 2 * np.pi * 50e6  # 50 MHz Rabi frequency
t = np.linspace(0, 40e-9, 1000)  # 0 to 40 ns

# Probability of |1⟩ state
P1 = np.sin(Omega * t / 2)**2

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.plot(t * 1e9, P1, 'b-', linewidth=2)
plt.axhline(y=0.5, color='gray', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
plt.axvline(x=5, color='r', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='π/2 pulse')
plt.axvline(x=10, color='g', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='π pulse')
plt.xlabel('Time (ns)')
plt.ylabel('P(|1⟩)')
plt.title('Rabi Oscillations at Ω/2π = 50 MHz')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
```
</Example>

### Off-Resonant Driving

When $\Delta \neq 0$, the effective Rabi frequency is enhanced:

<Formula>
$$\Omega_{eff} = \sqrt{\Omega^2 + \Delta^2}$$
</Formula>

And the maximum excitation probability is reduced:

<Formula>
$$P_1^{max} = \frac{\Omega^2}{\Omega^2 + \Delta^2}$$
</Formula>

<Tip>
Detuned driving is useful for implementing Z rotations (via AC Stark shift) without fully exciting the qubit, and for frequency-selective addressing of specific qubits.
</Tip>

## 5. Implementing Quantum Gates

### X and Y Gates

The standard Pauli gates are implemented using resonant π pulses:

**X Gate (π pulse with φ=0):**
<Formula>
$$X = e^{-i\frac{\pi}{2}\sigma_x} = -i\sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ -i & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
</Formula>

**Y Gate (π pulse with φ=π/2):**
<Formula>
$$Y = e^{-i\frac{\pi}{2}\sigma_y} = -i\sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$
</Formula>

### Arbitrary Rotations

For a rotation by angle θ about an axis in the XY plane at angle φ from X:

<Formula>
$$R_{\phi}(\theta) = e^{-i\frac{\theta}{2}(\cos\phi\,\sigma_x + \sin\phi\,\sigma_y)}$$
</Formula>

This requires:
- Pulse duration: $t = \theta/\Omega$
- Drive phase: $\phi$

### Z Rotations

Z rotations can be implemented in two ways:

**1. Virtual Z Gates (software):**
<Formula>
$$R_z(\theta) = e^{-i\frac{\theta}{2}\sigma_z}$$
</Formula>

Simply shift the phase reference for all subsequent pulses by θ. This is instantaneous and error-free!

**2. Detuned Pulses (hardware):**

Apply a far-detuned pulse that induces an AC Stark shift:
<Formula>
$$\omega_{Stark} = \frac{\Omega^2}{4\Delta}$$
</Formula>

<Example>
```python
# Qiskit Pulse example for X90 gate
from qiskit import pulse
from qiskit.pulse import DriveChannel, GaussianSquare

# Define a π/2 rotation (X90 gate)
with pulse.build(name='x90_pulse') as x90_schedule:
    drive_chan = DriveChannel(0)

    # Gaussian-square pulse parameters
    duration = 160  # samples (at ~0.22 ns/sample ≈ 35 ns)
    amplitude = 0.1  # normalized amplitude
    sigma = 40  # Gaussian rise/fall width

    pulse.play(
        GaussianSquare(duration=duration, amp=amplitude,
                       sigma=sigma, width=80),
        drive_chan
    )

# For Y90, add phase shift
with pulse.build(name='y90_pulse') as y90_schedule:
    drive_chan = DriveChannel(0)
    pulse.shift_phase(np.pi/2, drive_chan)  # Virtual Z
    pulse.play(
        GaussianSquare(duration=160, amp=0.1, sigma=40, width=80),
        drive_chan
    )
    pulse.shift_phase(-np.pi/2, drive_chan)  # Undo virtual Z
```
</Example>

## 6. Pulse Amplitude and Calibration

### Determining the Drive Amplitude

The Rabi frequency Ω depends on the drive amplitude V:

<Formula>
$$\Omega = \frac{2e \cdot V \cdot n_{01}}{\hbar}$$
</Formula>

where the proportionality constant depends on:
- Coupling capacitance to the drive line
- Attenuation in the cryogenic wiring
- Qubit charge matrix element

<Definition term="Rabi Rate Calibration">
Rabi rate calibration is the experimental procedure to determine the relationship between the control voltage/DAC amplitude and the resulting Rabi frequency. This is essential for achieving accurate gate rotations.
</Definition>

### Calibration Procedure

1. **Rabi Experiment**: Apply pulses of varying duration at fixed amplitude
2. **Fit Oscillations**: Extract the Rabi frequency from the oscillation period
3. **Amplitude Scaling**: Determine Ω vs amplitude relationship
4. **Verify Gates**: Confirm π and π/2 pulses produce correct state populations

```python
# Rabi calibration analysis
def analyze_rabi(durations, populations, guess_freq=50e6):
    """
    Fit Rabi oscillation data to extract Rabi frequency.

    Args:
        durations: Array of pulse durations (s)
        populations: Measured |1⟩ populations
        guess_freq: Initial guess for Rabi frequency (Hz)

    Returns:
        Fitted Rabi frequency and decay rate
    """
    from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

    def rabi_model(t, Omega, T_decay, offset, amplitude):
        return offset + amplitude * np.sin(Omega * t / 2)**2 * np.exp(-t / T_decay)

    p0 = [2 * np.pi * guess_freq, 1e-6, 0.0, 1.0]
    popt, pcov = curve_fit(rabi_model, durations, populations, p0=p0)

    Omega_fit = popt[0]
    T_decay = popt[1]

    return {
        'rabi_frequency_hz': Omega_fit / (2 * np.pi),
        'pi_time_ns': np.pi / Omega_fit * 1e9,
        'decay_time_us': T_decay * 1e6
    }
```

## 7. Frequency Considerations

### Qubit Frequency Tracking

Qubit frequencies can drift due to:
- Temperature fluctuations
- Two-level system (TLS) defects
- Quasi-particle tunneling
- Flux noise (for tunable qubits)

<Warning>
Frequency drift causes the drive to become detuned, reducing gate fidelity. Modern systems implement active frequency tracking through Ramsey experiments performed periodically during computation.
</Warning>

### Frequency Calibration (Ramsey Experiment)

The Ramsey experiment measures the qubit frequency precisely:

1. Apply π/2 pulse
2. Wait time τ with drive off (but at detuning Δ)
3. Apply second π/2 pulse
4. Measure

The resulting fringes oscillate at the detuning frequency:

<Formula>
$$P_1(\tau) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \cos(\Delta \cdot \tau)e^{-\tau/T_2^*}\right)$$
</Formula>

## 8. Summary

Microwave control of superconducting qubits relies on several key principles:

1. **Charge Coupling**: Microwave fields couple to qubits through the charge operator
2. **Rotating Frame**: Transforms the problem to remove fast oscillations at ωq
3. **Rotating Wave Approximation**: Valid for Ω << ωq, simplifies dynamics
4. **Rabi Oscillations**: Coherent oscillations between |0⟩ and |1⟩
5. **Gate Implementation**: X, Y rotations via resonant pulses; Z via virtual frame updates
6. **Calibration**: Essential for relating control amplitudes to rotation angles

Understanding these fundamentals is essential for designing high-fidelity quantum gates and working at the pulse level of quantum control.

<Tip>
Modern quantum processors achieve single-qubit gate fidelities above 99.9% through careful pulse design and calibration. The techniques in this lesson form the foundation for all subsequent pulse-level optimizations.
</Tip>
