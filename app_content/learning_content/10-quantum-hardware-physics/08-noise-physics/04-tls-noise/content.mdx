## 1. Introduction to Two-Level Systems

Two-level systems (TLS) are ubiquitous defects in amorphous materials that have plagued low-temperature physics experiments for decades. In quantum computing, they represent one of the most significant obstacles to achieving long coherence times.

<Definition term="Two-Level System (TLS)">
A two-level system is a defect (often an atom or group of atoms) that can tunnel quantum mechanically between two nearly degenerate configurations. At low temperatures, only the ground and first excited states are thermally accessible, creating an effective two-level system that can couple to qubits.
</Definition>

### Why TLS Matter

TLS defects create problems through multiple mechanisms:
- **Resonant absorption**: TLS at the qubit frequency cause direct energy loss
- **Dispersive shifts**: Off-resonant TLS cause frequency noise as they fluctuate
- **1/f noise**: Ensemble of TLS with distributed parameters produces 1/f spectrum
- **Telegraph noise**: Individual strongly-coupled TLS cause discrete frequency jumps

## 2. The Standard Tunneling Model

### Physical Picture

<Formula>
$$H_{TLS} = \frac{\Delta_0}{2}\sigma_x + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\sigma_z$$
</Formula>

where:
- $\Delta_0$ is the tunnel splitting (coupling between wells)
- $\epsilon$ is the asymmetry energy (difference between well depths)

The energy splitting is:

<Formula>
$$E = \sqrt{\Delta_0^2 + \epsilon^2}$$
</Formula>

### Distribution of Parameters

The standard tunneling model assumes:
- Uniform distribution of asymmetry: $P(\epsilon) = \text{const}$
- Log-uniform distribution of tunnel splitting: $P(\Delta_0) \propto 1/\Delta_0$

This leads to a density of states:

<Formula>
$$n(E) = \frac{P_0}{\sqrt{E^2 - \Delta_0^2}}$$
</Formula>

where $P_0 \sim 10^{44} - 10^{46}$ J$^{-1}$m$^{-3}$ is the characteristic TLS density.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def tls_energy_splitting(Delta_0, epsilon):
    """Calculate TLS energy splitting."""
    return np.sqrt(Delta_0**2 + epsilon**2)

def tls_density_of_states(E, Delta_0_min, P_0):
    """
    TLS density of states in standard tunneling model.

    n(E) = P_0 / sqrt(E^2 - Delta_0^2) for E > Delta_0
    """
    if E < Delta_0_min:
        return 0
    return P_0 / np.sqrt(E**2 - Delta_0_min**2)

# Plot TLS distribution
Delta_0_values = np.logspace(-3, 0, 50)  # GHz
epsilon_values = np.linspace(-5, 5, 100)  # GHz

# Create 2D distribution
Delta_mesh, eps_mesh = np.meshgrid(Delta_0_values, epsilon_values)
E_mesh = tls_energy_splitting(Delta_mesh, eps_mesh)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.contourf(Delta_0_values, epsilon_values, E_mesh, levels=20, cmap='viridis')
plt.colorbar(label='Energy splitting (GHz)')
plt.xlabel('Tunnel splitting Delta_0 (GHz)')
plt.ylabel('Asymmetry epsilon (GHz)')
plt.xscale('log')
plt.title('TLS Energy Splitting in Parameter Space')
plt.show()
```

## 3. TLS-Qubit Coupling

### Coupling Mechanisms

TLS couple to qubits through the electric field:

<Formula>
$$H_{int} = \mathbf{d}_{TLS} \cdot \mathbf{E}_{qubit}$$
</Formula>

where $\mathbf{d}_{TLS}$ is the TLS electric dipole moment (typically 1-5 Debye).

The coupling strength is:

<Formula>
$$g = \frac{d \cdot E_{vac}}{2\hbar} \sin\theta$$
</Formula>

where $\theta = \arctan(\Delta_0/\epsilon)$ and $E_{vac}$ is the vacuum electric field of the qubit mode.

<Tip>
The coupling depends on the "mixing angle" theta. TLS with large tunnel splitting (Delta_0 >> epsilon) couple strongly, while highly asymmetric TLS (epsilon >> Delta_0) have suppressed coupling.
</Tip>

### Resonant vs Dispersive Regime

For a TLS at frequency $\omega_{TLS}$ interacting with a qubit at $\omega_q$:

**Resonant** ($|\omega_{TLS} - \omega_q| < g$):
- Strong hybridization
- Avoided crossing visible in spectroscopy
- Energy exchange on timescale $1/g$

**Dispersive** ($|\omega_{TLS} - \omega_q| \gg g$):
- Weak perturbation
- Frequency shift: $\delta\omega = \pm g^2/\Delta$
- No direct energy exchange

## 4. TLS-Induced Decoherence

### T1 from Resonant TLS

The T1 relaxation rate from a bath of TLS is:

<Formula>
$$\Gamma_1 = \frac{\pi}{\hbar^2} |g|^2 n_{TLS}(\omega_q) \tanh\left(\frac{\hbar\omega_q}{2k_BT}\right)$$
</Formula>

where $n_{TLS}(\omega_q)$ is the TLS density of states at the qubit frequency.

### Dephasing from Fluctuating TLS

Off-resonant TLS that fluctuate between states cause phase noise:

<Formula>
$$S_\omega(f) = \sum_i \frac{4(\delta\omega_i)^2 \tau_i}{1 + (2\pi f \tau_i)^2}$$
</Formula>

where $\delta\omega_i = g_i^2/\Delta_i$ is the dispersive shift from TLS $i$ and $\tau_i$ is its switching time.

<Warning>
The sum over many TLS with distributed parameters produces 1/f noise! This is the Dutta-Horn mechanism we studied earlier, now with a concrete microscopic origin.
</Warning>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def tls_decoherence_rates(qubit_freq_GHz, n_tls, g_mean_MHz, T_mK, detuning_range_GHz=2):
    """
    Calculate T1 and T_phi contributions from TLS bath.

    Args:
        qubit_freq_GHz: Qubit frequency
        n_tls: Number of TLS per GHz bandwidth
        g_mean_MHz: Mean coupling strength
        T_mK: Temperature in mK
        detuning_range_GHz: Range to sum over for dispersive TLS
    """
    h = 6.626e-34
    kB = 1.381e-23
    hbar = h / (2 * np.pi)

    omega_q = qubit_freq_GHz * 1e9 * 2 * np.pi
    T = T_mK * 1e-3
    g_mean = g_mean_MHz * 1e6 * 2 * np.pi

    # T1 from resonant TLS
    # Assume TLS at exactly qubit frequency
    # Gamma_1 ~ g^2 * n_TLS * tanh(hbar*omega/2kT)
    tanh_factor = np.tanh(hbar * omega_q / (2 * kB * T)) if T > 0 else 1

    # Estimate: if n_tls resonant TLS with coupling g
    Gamma_1_resonant = g_mean**2 * n_tls * tanh_factor / (qubit_freq_GHz * 1e9)
    T1_resonant_us = 1e6 / Gamma_1_resonant if Gamma_1_resonant > 0 else np.inf

    # Dephasing from off-resonant TLS
    # Each TLS causes shift delta_omega = g^2/Delta
    # For many TLS: pure dephasing rate ~ sum of (delta_omega)^2 * switching_rate
    # Simplified estimate
    typical_detuning = 0.5e9 * 2 * np.pi  # 500 MHz typical detuning
    delta_omega = g_mean**2 / typical_detuning
    n_dispersive = n_tls * detuning_range_GHz

    # Assume TLS switching rate of 1 kHz typical
    gamma_switch = 1e3
    Gamma_phi = n_dispersive * delta_omega**2 * gamma_switch

    T_phi_us = 1e6 / Gamma_phi if Gamma_phi > 0 else np.inf

    return {
        'T1_resonant_us': T1_resonant_us,
        'T_phi_dispersive_us': T_phi_us,
        'Gamma_1': Gamma_1_resonant,
        'Gamma_phi': Gamma_phi,
        'n_resonant_TLS': n_tls,
        'n_dispersive_TLS': n_dispersive
    }

# Example calculation
result = tls_decoherence_rates(
    qubit_freq_GHz=5,
    n_tls=10,  # 10 TLS per GHz
    g_mean_MHz=5,
    T_mK=20
)

print("TLS Decoherence Analysis:")
for key, value in result.items():
    print(f"  {key}: {value:.2e}")
```

## 5. TLS Spectroscopy Signatures

### Avoided Crossings

Strongly-coupled TLS appear as avoided crossings in two-tone spectroscopy:

- Qubit frequency splits when tuned through TLS resonance
- Splitting magnitude gives coupling strength: $2g$
- TLS linewidth visible in the splitting shape

### TLS Hunting

Protocol to identify and characterize TLS:
1. Sweep qubit frequency (via flux)
2. Monitor T1 vs frequency
3. Sharp T1 dips indicate resonant TLS
4. Two-tone spectroscopy reveals avoided crossing

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def simulate_tls_avoided_crossing(qubit_freq_range_GHz, tls_freq_GHz, g_MHz):
    """
    Simulate qubit spectroscopy with TLS avoided crossing.

    Returns hybridized frequencies vs qubit flux/frequency tuning.
    """
    omega_q = qubit_freq_range_GHz * 2 * np.pi  # GHz
    omega_tls = tls_freq_GHz * 2 * np.pi
    g = g_MHz * 1e-3 * 2 * np.pi  # Convert MHz to GHz

    # Hybridized frequencies from diagonalizing 2x2 Hamiltonian
    delta = omega_q - omega_tls  # Detuning

    omega_plus = 0.5 * (omega_q + omega_tls + np.sqrt(delta**2 + 4*g**2))
    omega_minus = 0.5 * (omega_q + omega_tls - np.sqrt(delta**2 + 4*g**2))

    return omega_plus / (2 * np.pi), omega_minus / (2 * np.pi)

# Simulate
qubit_freq = np.linspace(4.5, 5.5, 200)
tls_freq = 5.0  # GHz
g = 20  # MHz

omega_plus, omega_minus = simulate_tls_avoided_crossing(qubit_freq, tls_freq, g)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(qubit_freq, omega_plus, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='Upper branch')
plt.plot(qubit_freq, omega_minus, 'r-', linewidth=2, label='Lower branch')
plt.plot(qubit_freq, qubit_freq, 'k--', alpha=0.3, label='Bare qubit')
plt.axhline(y=tls_freq, color='gray', linestyle=':', alpha=0.5, label='TLS frequency')

plt.xlabel('Bare qubit frequency (GHz)')
plt.ylabel('Dressed frequency (GHz)')
plt.title(f'TLS Avoided Crossing (g = {g} MHz)')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

print(f"Minimum splitting: {(min(omega_plus - omega_minus))*1000:.1f} MHz = 2g")
```

## 6. TLS Saturation

### Power Dependence

At high drive power, TLS can be saturated, reducing their effect:

<Formula>
$$T_1(P) = T_1^0 + \frac{T_1^{sat}}{1 + P/P_c}$$
</Formula>

where $P_c$ is the critical power for saturation.

<Definition term="TLS Saturation">
At high photon numbers, TLS are driven into a mixed state where they cannot absorb energy. This effectively removes them as loss mechanisms. Saturation occurs when the drive rate exceeds the TLS relaxation rate.
</Definition>

### Implications for Qubit Operation

- **Readout**: High-power readout can saturate TLS, improving T1 during readout
- **Gate calibration**: Pulse amplitude affects effective T1
- **Resonator Q**: Q increases with power due to TLS saturation

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def t1_power_dependence(P_dBm, T1_0_us, T1_sat_us, P_c_dBm):
    """
    T1 vs power with TLS saturation.

    T1(P) = T1_0 + T1_sat / (1 + P/P_c)
    """
    P = 10**(P_dBm / 10)
    P_c = 10**(P_c_dBm / 10)

    return T1_0_us + T1_sat_us / (1 + P / P_c)

# Plot T1 vs power
P_range = np.linspace(-140, -80, 100)  # dBm
T1_0 = 50  # us (non-TLS contribution)
T1_sat = 100  # us (TLS contribution at low power)
P_c = -120  # dBm (critical power)

T1_values = t1_power_dependence(P_range, T1_0, T1_sat, P_c)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(P_range, T1_values, 'b-', linewidth=2)
plt.axhline(y=T1_0, color='gray', linestyle='--', label=f'Non-TLS limit ({T1_0} us)')
plt.axhline(y=T1_0 + T1_sat, color='red', linestyle=':', label=f'Low-power limit ({T1_0 + T1_sat} us)')
plt.axvline(x=P_c, color='green', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label=f'P_c = {P_c} dBm')

plt.xlabel('Drive power (dBm)')
plt.ylabel('T1 (us)')
plt.title('T1 vs Drive Power: TLS Saturation Effect')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
```

## 7. Materials Strategies for TLS Reduction

### Sources of TLS

TLS are primarily located at interfaces:
- Metal-substrate interface
- Metal-air interface (oxide layer)
- Substrate-air interface

The "participation ratio" quantifies how much electric field energy is stored in each region.

### Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | Mechanism | Improvement |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Substrate cleaning | Remove surface contaminants | 2-5x |
| Tantalum instead of Al | Better oxide quality | 2-10x |
| Epitaxial growth | Eliminate amorphous interfaces | 5-20x |
| Undercuts | Reduce substrate participation | 2-5x |
| Encapsulation | Protect from air exposure | Variable |

### Participation Ratio Engineering

<Formula>
$$Q^{-1} = \sum_i p_i \cdot \tan\delta_i$$
</Formula>

where $p_i$ is the participation ratio and $\tan\delta_i$ is the loss tangent of region $i$.

<Tip>
Reducing participation ratios in lossy regions (like oxide surfaces) is often more practical than improving the material quality. Modern 3D transmon designs achieve this by moving the electric field away from surfaces.
</Tip>

## 8. Summary

Two-level systems are the microscopic origin of much noise in superconducting qubits:

1. **Standard tunneling model**: Atoms tunnel between configurations
2. **Coupling**: Electric dipole interaction with qubit field
3. **Effects**: T1 loss (resonant), dephasing (dispersive), 1/f noise (ensemble)
4. **Signatures**: Avoided crossings, T1 vs frequency, power dependence
5. **Mitigation**: Materials improvements, reduce participation ratios

Key numbers:
- TLS density: $10^{44} - 10^{46}$ J$^{-1}$m$^{-3}$
- Typical coupling: 1-50 MHz
- Dipole moment: 1-5 Debye

<Warning>
TLS remain one of the biggest challenges in superconducting qubits. While significant progress has been made (T1 improved from ~1 us to >500 us over 20 years), TLS continue to limit coherence in state-of-the-art devices.
</Warning>
