## 1. Introduction to 1/f Noise

1/f noise, also called "pink noise" or "flicker noise," is perhaps the most universal yet mysterious phenomenon in physics. It appears in systems ranging from electronic devices to heartbeat intervals, from stock markets to qubit coherence times.

<Definition term="1/f Noise">
1/f noise is characterized by a power spectral density that scales inversely with frequency: S(f) = A/f^alpha, where alpha is close to 1 (typically 0.8 to 1.2). This means low-frequency fluctuations have much more power than high-frequency ones.
</Definition>

### Why 1/f Noise Matters for Qubits

In superconducting qubits, 1/f noise is the dominant source of pure dephasing (T_phi), limiting T2* to values much shorter than the T1-limit. Understanding and mitigating 1/f noise is essential for achieving high-fidelity quantum operations.

<Formula>
$$S(f) = \frac{A_{1/f}}{|f|^\alpha}$$
</Formula>

where $A_{1/f}$ is the noise amplitude and $\alpha \approx 1$ is the noise exponent.

## 2. Physical Origins of 1/f Noise

### The Dutta-Horn Model: Superposition of Fluctuators

The most successful model for 1/f noise involves a superposition of two-level fluctuators (TLFs) with a distribution of switching rates.

<Definition term="Dutta-Horn Model">
The Dutta-Horn model explains 1/f noise as arising from an ensemble of two-level systems (TLS), each producing Lorentzian telegraph noise. When the switching rates are distributed as 1/tau, the sum produces 1/f noise.
</Definition>

For a single TLS with switching rate $\gamma$, the spectrum is Lorentzian:

<Formula>
$$S_{TLS}(f) = \frac{4\delta\nu^2 \tau}{1 + (2\pi f \tau)^2}$$
</Formula>

where $\delta\nu$ is the frequency shift caused by the TLS and $\tau = 1/\gamma$ is the correlation time.

Summing over many TLS with distributed rates:

<Formula>
$$S(f) = \int_{\tau_{min}}^{\tau_{max}} S_{TLS}(f, \tau) P(\tau) d\tau$$
</Formula>

With $P(\tau) \propto 1/\tau$ (uniform distribution of activation energies):

<Formula>
$$S(f) \approx \frac{A_{1/f}}{f} \quad \text{for} \quad \frac{1}{\tau_{max}} \ll f \ll \frac{1}{\tau_{min}}$$
</Formula>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def lorentzian_spectrum(f, delta_nu, tau):
    """Single TLS Lorentzian spectrum."""
    return 4 * delta_nu**2 * tau / (1 + (2 * np.pi * f * tau)**2)

def sum_of_tls_spectrum(f, delta_nu, tau_min, tau_max, n_tls=100):
    """
    Sum of TLS spectra with log-uniform distribution of tau.
    This produces 1/f noise.
    """
    # Log-uniform distribution of tau values
    taus = np.logspace(np.log10(tau_min), np.log10(tau_max), n_tls)

    S_total = np.zeros_like(f)
    for tau in taus:
        S_total += lorentzian_spectrum(f, delta_nu, tau) / n_tls

    return S_total

# Generate spectrum
f = np.logspace(-3, 6, 1000)  # Hz
delta_nu = 1e3  # Hz
tau_min = 1e-6  # s
tau_max = 1e3   # s

S = sum_of_tls_spectrum(f, delta_nu, tau_min, tau_max, n_tls=1000)

# Plot
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.loglog(f, S, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='Sum of TLS')
plt.loglog(f, 1e9/f, 'r--', linewidth=2, label='1/f reference')
plt.xlabel('Frequency (Hz)')
plt.ylabel('S(f) (Hz^2/Hz)')
plt.title('1/f Noise from Superposition of Two-Level Fluctuators')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xlim(1e-2, 1e5)
plt.show()
```

### Sources in Superconducting Qubits

#### Flux Noise

Magnetic flux noise has 1/f spectrum with typical amplitude:

<Formula>
$$S_\Phi(f) = \frac{A_\Phi^2}{f}$$
</Formula>

where $A_\Phi \approx 1-10 \, \mu\Phi_0/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at 1 Hz.

<Tip>
Flux noise couples to the qubit through the SQUID loop in tunable transmons. The dephasing rate scales with the flux sensitivity: $\Gamma_\phi \propto (d\omega_q/d\Phi)^2 \cdot S_\Phi$.
</Tip>

#### Charge Noise

Charge noise affects qubits through the gate capacitance:

<Formula>
$$S_q(f) = \frac{A_q^2}{f}$$
</Formula>

where $A_q \approx 10^{-4} - 10^{-3} \, e/\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at 1 Hz.

For transmons with large $E_J/E_C$, charge noise sensitivity is exponentially suppressed.

## 3. Dephasing from 1/f Noise

### The Gaussian Approximation

For 1/f noise, the accumulated phase during free evolution is Gaussian-distributed with variance:

<Formula>
$$\sigma_\phi^2(t) = \int_0^\infty \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} S_z(\omega) |F(\omega, t)|^2 / \omega^2$$
</Formula>

For free induction decay (Ramsey) with 1/f noise:

<Formula>
$$\sigma_\phi^2(t) = A_{1/f} \left[ \ln\left(\frac{t}{t_{IR}}\right) + \gamma_E \right] t^2$$
</Formula>

where $t_{IR}$ is an infrared cutoff time and $\gamma_E \approx 0.577$ is Euler's constant.

### Gaussian Dephasing

The coherence decays as:

<Formula>
$$W(t) = e^{-\sigma_\phi^2(t)/2} = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}A_{1/f} t^2 \ln(t/t_{IR})\right)$$
</Formula>

At short times, this is approximately Gaussian:

<Formula>
$$W(t) \approx e^{-(t/T_\phi^*)^2}$$
</Formula>

<Warning>
Unlike exponential decay from white noise, 1/f noise produces Gaussian decay at short times. This is a key signature of non-Markovian, low-frequency noise dominance.
</Warning>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def coherence_1f_noise(t, A_1f, t_ir=1e-9):
    """
    Coherence decay from pure 1/f noise.

    Args:
        t: Time array
        A_1f: 1/f noise amplitude (rad^2)
        t_ir: Infrared cutoff time
    """
    # Avoid log of zero
    t = np.maximum(t, t_ir)

    sigma_sq = A_1f * t**2 * (np.log(t / t_ir) + 0.577)
    return np.exp(-sigma_sq / 2)

def coherence_white_noise(t, Gamma):
    """Exponential decay from white noise."""
    return np.exp(-Gamma * t)

# Compare decay profiles
t = np.linspace(1e-9, 50e-6, 500)

# Matched at t = 10 us for comparison
t_match = 10e-6
A_1f = 1e9  # Adjusted for T2* ~ 10 us
Gamma = 1 / 10e-6

W_1f = coherence_1f_noise(t, A_1f)
W_white = coherence_white_noise(t, Gamma)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.semilogy(t * 1e6, W_1f, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='1/f noise (Gaussian)')
plt.semilogy(t * 1e6, W_white, 'r--', linewidth=2, label='White noise (exponential)')
plt.xlabel('Time (us)')
plt.ylabel('Coherence')
plt.title('Dephasing: 1/f Noise vs White Noise')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.ylim(1e-3, 1)
plt.show()

# The curvature difference is key:
# 1/f noise: d^2W/dt^2 > 0 near t=0 (Gaussian)
# White noise: d^2W/dt^2 = 0 (exponential)
```

## 4. Measuring 1/f Noise

### Ramsey Experiments

The Ramsey experiment is sensitive to low-frequency noise:

1. Apply $\pi/2$ pulse
2. Wait time $\tau$
3. Apply second $\pi/2$ pulse
4. Measure

<Example>
Extracting T2* and noise amplitude from Ramsey decay:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import curve_fit

def ramsey_model_1f(t, A, T2_star, f_detuning, phi_0, B):
    """
    Ramsey decay with Gaussian envelope (1/f noise) and oscillation.

    W(t) = A * exp(-(t/T2*)^2) * cos(2*pi*f*t + phi_0) + B
    """
    envelope = A * np.exp(-(t / T2_star)**2)
    oscillation = np.cos(2 * np.pi * f_detuning * t + phi_0)
    return envelope * oscillation + B

def fit_ramsey_1f(times, signal):
    """
    Fit Ramsey data assuming 1/f noise dominates.

    Returns T2* and estimated noise amplitude.
    """
    # FFT to estimate detuning
    dt = times[1] - times[0]
    fft = np.fft.fft(signal - np.mean(signal))
    freqs = np.fft.fftfreq(len(times), dt)
    peak_idx = np.argmax(np.abs(fft[:len(fft)//2]))
    f_guess = np.abs(freqs[peak_idx])

    # Initial guesses
    p0 = [0.4, times[-1]/3, f_guess, 0, 0.5]
    bounds = ([0, 1e-9, 0, -np.pi, 0], [0.5, times[-1]*2, 1e7, np.pi, 1])

    popt, pcov = curve_fit(ramsey_model_1f, times, signal, p0=p0, bounds=bounds)

    A, T2_star, f_det, phi, B = popt
    errors = np.sqrt(np.diag(pcov))

    # Convert T2* to noise amplitude
    # For 1/f: T2* ~ 1/sqrt(A_1f * ln(T2*/t_ir))
    # Approximate: A_1f ~ 1/T2*^2 (ignoring log factor)
    A_1f_estimate = 1 / T2_star**2

    return {
        'T2_star': T2_star,
        'T2_star_error': errors[1],
        'detuning_Hz': f_det,
        'amplitude': A,
        'offset': B,
        'A_1f_estimate': A_1f_estimate
    }

# Generate synthetic Ramsey data with 1/f noise
np.random.seed(42)
times = np.linspace(0, 30e-6, 100)
T2_star_true = 8e-6
f_det_true = 100e3

signal_true = 0.45 * np.exp(-(times/T2_star_true)**2) * np.cos(2*np.pi*f_det_true*times) + 0.5
signal = signal_true + 0.02 * np.random.randn(len(times))

result = fit_ramsey_1f(times, signal)
print(f"Fitted T2*: {result['T2_star']*1e6:.2f} us")
print(f"Detuning: {result['detuning_Hz']/1e3:.1f} kHz")
print(f"Estimated A_1f: {result['A_1f_estimate']:.2e} rad^2")
```
</Example>

### Echo Experiments for Spectroscopy

The Hahn echo and CPMG sequences can probe the noise spectrum:

<Formula>
$$T_2^{(n)} \propto \left(\frac{A_{1/f}}{\omega_n^{1-\alpha}}\right)^{-1}$$
</Formula>

where $\omega_n = n\pi/t$ is the frequency probed by $n$ refocusing pulses.

For ideal 1/f noise ($\alpha = 1$):

<Formula>
$$T_2^{echo} \propto n$$
</Formula>

Echo time should scale linearly with the number of pulses!

## 5. Mitigating 1/f Noise

### Dynamical Decoupling

Dynamical decoupling sequences apply periodic $\pi$ pulses to refocus low-frequency noise. The filter function suppresses noise at frequencies below $\omega_c \sim \pi/\tau$, where $\tau$ is the pulse spacing.

<Definition term="CPMG Sequence">
The Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) sequence consists of equally spaced pi pulses: [pi/2]_x - [tau - pi_y - tau]^n - [measure]. It efficiently suppresses low-frequency noise while being robust to pulse errors.
</Definition>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def cpmg_filter_function(omega, t_total, n_pulses):
    """
    CPMG filter function |F(omega)|^2.

    For n pulses over time t_total:
    F(omega) peaks at omega_k = k * pi * n / t_total
    """
    if n_pulses == 0:
        # Free evolution (Ramsey)
        return 4 * np.sin(omega * t_total / 2)**2

    tau = t_total / (2 * n_pulses)

    # Approximate CPMG filter (simplified form)
    # Full expression involves sums of sines
    result = np.zeros_like(omega)
    for w_idx, w in enumerate(omega):
        if w == 0:
            result[w_idx] = 0
            continue

        # Filter function for CPMG
        x = w * tau
        if n_pulses % 2 == 0:
            result[w_idx] = 8 * np.sin(x/2)**4 * np.sin(n_pulses*x/2)**2 / np.cos(x/2)**2
        else:
            result[w_idx] = 8 * np.sin(x/2)**4 * np.cos(n_pulses*x/2)**2 / np.cos(x/2)**2

    return result

# Compare filter functions
t_total = 10e-6
omega = np.linspace(0.01e6, 10e6, 1000)

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 5))

# Different number of CPMG pulses
for n in [0, 1, 2, 4, 8]:
    F_sq = cpmg_filter_function(omega, t_total, n)
    label = 'Ramsey' if n == 0 else f'CPMG-{n}'
    plt.semilogy(omega / (2*np.pi*1e6), F_sq / np.max(F_sq),
                 label=label, linewidth=2)

plt.xlabel('Frequency (MHz)')
plt.ylabel('|F(omega)|^2 (normalized)')
plt.title('Filter Functions: Ramsey vs CPMG')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xlim(0, 1.5)
plt.ylim(1e-4, 2)
plt.show()
```

### Optimal Working Points

For flux-tunable qubits, operating at a "sweet spot" where $d\omega_q/d\Phi = 0$ eliminates first-order sensitivity to flux noise:

<Formula>
$$\omega_q(\Phi) = \omega_{max} |\cos(\pi\Phi/\Phi_0)|$$
</Formula>

Sweet spots occur at $\Phi = 0$ and $\Phi = \Phi_0/2$.

<Tip>
At the sweet spot, only second-order noise couples to the qubit, dramatically reducing dephasing. However, the $\Phi = \Phi_0/2$ sweet spot has reduced anharmonicity and may be susceptible to other noise sources.
</Tip>

### Materials Improvements

Reducing the density of TLS defects:
- Better substrate treatment (annealing, cleaning)
- Cleaner metal deposition
- Reduced interface participation
- Tantalum instead of aluminum for capacitor pads

## 6. Advanced Topics

### Temperature Dependence

1/f noise amplitude often shows weak temperature dependence:

<Formula>
$$A_{1/f}(T) = A_0 \coth\left(\frac{E}{2k_BT}\right)$$
</Formula>

where $E$ is a characteristic TLS energy scale.

At very low temperatures ($k_BT \ll E$), the noise saturates due to quantum tunneling of TLS.

### Correlations Between Qubits

1/f noise can be correlated between nearby qubits:

<Formula>
$$\langle \delta\omega_i(t) \delta\omega_j(t') \rangle = S_{ij}(t-t')$$
</Formula>

Correlated noise poses challenges for error correction but can potentially be exploited through decoherence-free subspaces.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def correlated_1f_noise(n_qubits, times, A_1f, correlation_matrix):
    """
    Generate correlated 1/f noise for multiple qubits.

    Args:
        n_qubits: Number of qubits
        times: Time array
        A_1f: Noise amplitude
        correlation_matrix: NxN correlation matrix

    Returns:
        Array of shape (n_qubits, len(times)) with correlated noise
    """
    n_t = len(times)
    dt = times[1] - times[0]

    # Generate uncorrelated 1/f noise in frequency domain
    freqs = np.fft.fftfreq(n_t, dt)
    # 1/f spectrum (with IR cutoff)
    f_ir = 0.1  # Hz
    S_f = A_1f / (np.abs(freqs) + f_ir)
    S_f[0] = 0  # Remove DC

    # Generate correlated noise using Cholesky decomposition
    L = np.linalg.cholesky(correlation_matrix)

    noise = np.zeros((n_qubits, n_t))
    for i in range(n_qubits):
        # Random phases
        phases = 2 * np.pi * np.random.rand(n_t)
        noise_fft = np.sqrt(S_f) * np.exp(1j * phases)
        noise[i] = np.real(np.fft.ifft(noise_fft))

    # Apply correlations
    noise_correlated = L @ noise

    return noise_correlated

# Example: Two qubits with 50% correlation
n_qubits = 2
times = np.linspace(0, 100e-6, 1000)
A_1f = 1e12
correlation = 0.5
corr_matrix = np.array([[1, correlation], [correlation, 1]])

noise = correlated_1f_noise(n_qubits, times, A_1f, corr_matrix)

# Plot
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 6))

ax1 = axes[0]
ax1.plot(times*1e6, noise[0]/1e6, 'b-', alpha=0.7, label='Qubit 1')
ax1.plot(times*1e6, noise[1]/1e6, 'r-', alpha=0.7, label='Qubit 2')
ax1.set_xlabel('Time (us)')
ax1.set_ylabel('Frequency shift (MHz)')
ax1.set_title('Correlated 1/f Noise on Two Qubits')
ax1.legend()
ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

ax2 = axes[1]
ax2.scatter(noise[0]/1e6, noise[1]/1e6, alpha=0.3, s=5)
ax2.set_xlabel('Qubit 1 shift (MHz)')
ax2.set_ylabel('Qubit 2 shift (MHz)')
ax2.set_title(f'Correlation plot (target correlation = {correlation})')
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax2.set_aspect('equal')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# Calculate actual correlation
actual_corr = np.corrcoef(noise[0], noise[1])[0, 1]
print(f"Actual correlation: {actual_corr:.2f}")
```

## 7. Summary

1/f noise is the dominant dephasing mechanism in solid-state qubits:

1. **Physical origin**: Ensemble of two-level fluctuators with distributed switching rates
2. **Spectrum**: $S(f) \propto 1/f^\alpha$ with $\alpha \approx 1$
3. **Dephasing**: Gaussian decay at short times, unlike exponential from white noise
4. **Measurement**: Ramsey gives T2*, echo sequences probe spectrum
5. **Mitigation**: Dynamical decoupling, sweet spots, materials improvements

Key formulas:
- Noise spectrum: $S(f) = A_{1/f}/f$
- Phase variance: $\sigma_\phi^2 \propto A_{1/f} t^2 \ln(t/t_{IR})$
- Echo improvement: $T_2^{(n)}/T_2^{(1)} \propto n$ for ideal 1/f noise

<Warning>
1/f noise cannot be completely eliminated - it is intrinsic to materials. The strategy is to: (1) reduce its amplitude through better materials, and (2) filter it through dynamical decoupling and operating at sweet spots.
</Warning>
