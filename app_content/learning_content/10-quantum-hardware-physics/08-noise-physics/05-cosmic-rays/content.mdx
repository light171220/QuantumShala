## 1. Introduction: Radiation from Space

Cosmic rays are high-energy particles from space that continuously bombard Earth's surface. While invisible in everyday life, they pose a significant challenge for quantum computers operating at the edge of sensitivity.

<Definition term="Cosmic Ray">
Cosmic rays are high-energy particles, primarily protons and atomic nuclei, originating from outside Earth's atmosphere. At sea level, the primary particles have interacted with the atmosphere to produce secondary particles including muons, neutrons, and gamma rays.
</Definition>

### Why Cosmic Rays Matter for Quantum Computing

A single cosmic ray impact can:
- Create millions of quasiparticles in superconducting devices
- Cause simultaneous errors across multiple qubits
- Temporarily destroy quantum coherence chip-wide
- Invalidate quantum error correction assumptions

## 2. Cosmic Ray Physics at Sea Level

### Sea Level Particle Flux

| Particle | Flux (particles/cm^2/s) | Energy Range | Primary Effect |
|----------|-------------------------|--------------|----------------|
| Muons | ~0.01 | ~4 GeV | Direct ionization |
| Neutrons | ~0.004 | 1-100 MeV | Nuclear recoils |
| Gamma rays | ~0.003 | 0.1-10 MeV | Photoelectric, Compton |
| Protons | ~0.0003 | ~1 GeV | Direct ionization |

### Energy Deposition

When a high-energy particle passes through matter, it deposits energy through ionization:

<Formula>
$$\frac{dE}{dx} = \frac{4\pi n_e z^2 e^4}{m_e v^2} \left[\ln\left(\frac{2m_e v^2}{I}\right) - \beta^2\right]$$
</Formula>

This Bethe formula gives the energy loss per unit length, typically ~2 MeV/(g/cm^2) for minimum ionizing particles.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def muon_flux_vs_depth(depth_mwe):
    """
    Muon flux vs depth underground.

    Depth in meters water equivalent (mwe).
    Flux decreases roughly exponentially.
    """
    # Empirical fit to muon flux underground
    # Approximately: flux ~ flux_0 * exp(-depth/lambda)
    flux_0 = 0.01  # particles/cm^2/s at surface
    lambda_mwe = 400  # attenuation length

    return flux_0 * np.exp(-depth / lambda_mwe)

# Cosmic ray event rate on a chip
def event_rate_chip(chip_area_mm2, flux_per_cm2_s):
    """Calculate events per second on a chip."""
    area_cm2 = chip_area_mm2 / 100  # Convert mm^2 to cm^2
    return flux_per_cm2_s * area_cm2

# Example for a 5mm x 5mm chip
chip_area = 25  # mm^2
muon_flux = 0.01  # per cm^2 per second

rate = event_rate_chip(chip_area, muon_flux)
print(f"Chip area: {chip_area} mm^2")
print(f"Muon flux at surface: {muon_flux} /cm^2/s")
print(f"Event rate: {rate:.3f} /s = {rate*3600:.1f} /hour")
print(f"Mean time between events: {1/rate:.0f} seconds")
```

## 3. Quasiparticle Generation

### The Impact Process

When an ionizing particle passes through the superconductor:

1. **Energy deposition**: Particle deposits ~keV to MeV of energy
2. **Phonon cascade**: Energy converts to high-frequency phonons
3. **Cooper pair breaking**: Phonons with energy > 2$\Delta$ break Cooper pairs
4. **Quasiparticle burst**: ~10^6 quasiparticles created per keV deposited

<Formula>
$$N_{qp} \approx \frac{E_{dep}}{2\Delta} \cdot \eta$$
</Formula>

where $E_{dep}$ is deposited energy, $\Delta \approx 0.18$ meV for aluminum, and $\eta \approx 0.6$ is the pair-breaking efficiency.

### Quasiparticle Diffusion

The quasiparticle burst diffuses across the chip:

<Formula>
$$n_{qp}(r, t) = \frac{N_0}{(4\pi D t)^{3/2}} e^{-r^2/(4Dt)} e^{-t/\tau_{qp}}$$
</Formula>

where $D \sim 20$ cm^2/s is the diffusion constant and $\tau_{qp} \sim 1$ ms is the quasiparticle lifetime.

<Warning>
Quasiparticles can travel millimeters across the chip before recombining, causing correlated errors on multiple qubits that may be physically separated.
</Warning>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def quasiparticle_density(r_mm, t_us, N_0=1e6, D_cm2_s=20, tau_us=1000):
    """
    Calculate quasiparticle density vs position and time.

    Args:
        r_mm: Distance from impact in mm
        t_us: Time after impact in microseconds
        N_0: Initial number of quasiparticles
        D_cm2_s: Diffusion constant in cm^2/s
        tau_us: Quasiparticle lifetime in us
    """
    r_cm = r_mm / 10
    t_s = t_us * 1e-6
    tau_s = tau_us * 1e-6

    # 2D diffusion on chip surface
    if t_s <= 0:
        return 0

    n = N_0 / (4 * np.pi * D_cm2_s * t_s) * np.exp(-r_cm**2 / (4 * D_cm2_s * t_s)) * np.exp(-t_s / tau_s)

    return n  # per cm^2

# Visualize diffusion
r = np.linspace(0.01, 5, 100)  # mm
times = [1, 10, 100, 1000]  # microseconds

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
for t in times:
    n = [quasiparticle_density(ri, t) for ri in r]
    plt.semilogy(r, n, label=f't = {t} us')

plt.xlabel('Distance from impact (mm)')
plt.ylabel('Quasiparticle density (cm^-2)')
plt.title('Quasiparticle Diffusion After Cosmic Ray Impact')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xlim(0, 5)
plt.show()
```

## 4. Impact on Qubit Coherence

### T1 Degradation from Quasiparticles

Quasiparticles cause additional T1 relaxation:

<Formula>
$$\Gamma_1^{qp} = \frac{x_{qp}}{2\tau_{qp}} \sqrt{\frac{2\Delta}{\hbar\omega_{01}}}$$
</Formula>

where $x_{qp} = n_{qp}/n_{cp}$ is the normalized quasiparticle density.

### Temporal Profile of an Event

A cosmic ray impact causes a characteristic "recovery" profile:

<Formula>
$$T_1(t) = \frac{T_1^0}{1 + T_1^0 \cdot \Gamma_1^{qp}(t)}$$
</Formula>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def t1_after_impact(t_us, T1_0_us=100, N_qp_0=1e6, tau_qp_us=500):
    """
    T1 recovery after cosmic ray impact.

    Args:
        t_us: Time after impact
        T1_0_us: Baseline T1
        N_qp_0: Initial quasiparticle burst size
        tau_qp_us: Quasiparticle lifetime
    """
    # Quasiparticle density decays exponentially
    # x_qp(t) = x_qp_0 * exp(-t/tau_qp)
    x_qp_0 = 1e-5  # Initial normalized density (very high after impact)
    x_qp = x_qp_0 * np.exp(-t_us / tau_qp_us)

    # T1 reduction factor
    # Gamma_1_qp proportional to x_qp
    Gamma_factor = x_qp / 1e-7  # Normalized to typical x_qp ~ 1e-7

    T1 = T1_0_us / (1 + Gamma_factor)

    return T1

# Plot T1 recovery
t = np.linspace(0, 5000, 500)  # microseconds
T1_values = [t1_after_impact(ti) for ti in t]

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(t/1000, T1_values, 'b-', linewidth=2)
plt.axhline(y=100, color='gray', linestyle='--', label='Baseline T1')
plt.xlabel('Time after impact (ms)')
plt.ylabel('T1 (us)')
plt.title('Qubit T1 Recovery After Cosmic Ray Impact')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

print(f"T1 immediately after impact: {t1_after_impact(0):.1f} us")
print(f"T1 after 1 ms: {t1_after_impact(1000):.1f} us")
print(f"T1 after 5 ms: {t1_after_impact(5000):.1f} us")
```

## 5. Correlated Errors

### Spatial Correlations

Cosmic ray impacts cause errors across multiple qubits simultaneously:

<Definition term="Correlated Error Burst">
A cosmic ray impact creates a burst of errors affecting multiple qubits within the quasiparticle diffusion range (typically 1-5 mm). These errors occur within a few microseconds of each other and violate the independent error assumption of most QEC codes.
</Definition>

### Impact on Quantum Error Correction

Standard QEC assumes:
- Errors are independent between qubits
- Error rate is constant over time
- Errors don't cluster in space or time

Cosmic ray events violate all three assumptions!

<Warning>
A single cosmic ray can corrupt an entire logical qubit by causing errors on multiple physical qubits simultaneously. The QEC threshold assumes independent errors - correlated bursts can push the effective error rate above threshold.
</Warning>

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def simulate_cosmic_ray_errors(n_qubits=50, qubit_spacing_mm=0.5,
                                impact_x_mm=2, impact_y_mm=2,
                                diffusion_range_mm=2):
    """
    Simulate which qubits are affected by a cosmic ray impact.

    Returns array of affected qubit indices.
    """
    # Create grid of qubits
    grid_size = int(np.sqrt(n_qubits))
    qubit_positions = []
    for i in range(grid_size):
        for j in range(grid_size):
            x = i * qubit_spacing_mm
            y = j * qubit_spacing_mm
            qubit_positions.append((x, y))

    # Find qubits within diffusion range of impact
    affected = []
    for idx, (x, y) in enumerate(qubit_positions):
        distance = np.sqrt((x - impact_x_mm)**2 + (y - impact_y_mm)**2)
        if distance < diffusion_range_mm:
            # Probability of error decreases with distance
            p_error = np.exp(-distance / (diffusion_range_mm / 2))
            if np.random.random() < p_error:
                affected.append(idx)

    return affected, qubit_positions

# Simulate multiple impacts
n_simulations = 1000
n_qubits = 49  # 7x7 grid
errors_per_event = []

for _ in range(n_simulations):
    impact_x = np.random.uniform(0, 3)
    impact_y = np.random.uniform(0, 3)
    affected, _ = simulate_cosmic_ray_errors(n_qubits, 0.5, impact_x, impact_y, 2)
    errors_per_event.append(len(affected))

print(f"Simulation of {n_simulations} cosmic ray impacts on {n_qubits}-qubit chip:")
print(f"  Mean errors per event: {np.mean(errors_per_event):.1f}")
print(f"  Max errors in one event: {max(errors_per_event)}")
print(f"  Events affecting >10 qubits: {sum(1 for e in errors_per_event if e > 10)}")
```

## 6. Detection and Mitigation

### Hardware Detection

Methods to detect cosmic ray events:
1. **Quasiparticle sensors**: Kinetic inductance detectors on chip
2. **Phonon detectors**: Transition-edge sensors at chip periphery
3. **Veto counters**: Scintillator panels around the refrigerator

### Software Mitigation

1. **Real-time detection**: Monitor all qubits for sudden T1 drops
2. **Event flagging**: Tag measurement rounds affected by impacts
3. **Soft reset**: Discard affected data, re-initialize qubits
4. **Adaptive QEC**: Increase code distance during recovery

```python
import numpy as np

def cosmic_ray_detection(T1_measurements, baseline_T1, threshold_factor=0.5):
    """
    Detect cosmic ray events from T1 monitoring.

    Args:
        T1_measurements: Array of (time, T1_value) tuples
        baseline_T1: Expected T1 value
        threshold_factor: Flag if T1 drops below this fraction of baseline

    Returns:
        List of detected event times
    """
    events = []
    threshold = baseline_T1 * threshold_factor

    in_event = False
    event_start = None

    for time, T1 in T1_measurements:
        if T1 < threshold and not in_event:
            # Start of event
            in_event = True
            event_start = time
        elif T1 >= threshold and in_event:
            # End of event
            in_event = False
            events.append((event_start, time))

    return events

# Example detection
np.random.seed(42)
times = np.linspace(0, 100, 1000)  # seconds
baseline = 100  # us

# Simulate T1 with occasional cosmic ray events
T1_values = baseline + 5 * np.random.randn(len(times))

# Add cosmic ray events
event_times = [20, 50, 80]  # seconds
for et in event_times:
    mask = (times > et) & (times < et + 5)
    T1_values[mask] = 10 + 20 * (times[mask] - et)  # Recovery

measurements = list(zip(times, T1_values))
detected = cosmic_ray_detection(measurements, baseline, 0.5)

print(f"Injected events at: {event_times} s")
print(f"Detected events: {len(detected)}")
for start, end in detected:
    print(f"  Event from {start:.1f}s to {end:.1f}s (duration: {end-start:.1f}s)")
```

## 7. Scaling Implications

### Event Rate vs Chip Size

As quantum processors scale up:

<Formula>
$$R_{events} = \Phi \cdot A_{chip}$$
</Formula>

| Chip Size | Event Rate | Mean Time Between Events |
|-----------|------------|-------------------------|
| 5 mm x 5 mm | 0.003/s | 6 minutes |
| 20 mm x 20 mm | 0.04/s | 25 seconds |
| 50 mm x 50 mm | 0.25/s | 4 seconds |

<Tip>
For large-scale quantum computers, cosmic ray mitigation is not optional. Either reduce event rate (shielding, underground) or detect and recover from events in real-time.
</Tip>

## 8. Summary

Cosmic rays pose a unique challenge for quantum computing:

1. **Physics**: High-energy particles create quasiparticle bursts
2. **Scale**: ~10^6 quasiparticles per keV deposited
3. **Diffusion**: Quasiparticles spread across millimeters
4. **Correlations**: Multiple qubits affected simultaneously
5. **QEC impact**: Violates independent error assumption
6. **Mitigation**: Detection, flagging, shielding, underground

Key numbers:
- Sea level muon flux: ~0.01/cm^2/s
- Quasiparticle lifetime: ~100-1000 us
- Recovery time: ~1-10 ms
- Diffusion range: ~1-5 mm

<Warning>
As quantum computers scale beyond ~100 qubits, cosmic ray mitigation becomes critical. Without it, error correction may be fundamentally limited by correlated error bursts.
</Warning>
