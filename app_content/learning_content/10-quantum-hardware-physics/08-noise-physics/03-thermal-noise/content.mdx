## 1. Introduction to Thermal Noise

Thermal noise is the most fundamental form of noise, arising from the random thermal motion of charge carriers. In quantum systems, it takes on new character as photon number fluctuations that can excite qubits and destroy quantum coherence.

<Definition term="Thermal Noise">
Thermal noise (also called Johnson-Nyquist noise) is the electronic noise generated by thermal agitation of charge carriers inside a conductor at equilibrium. In quantum systems, it manifests as thermal population of photon modes according to the Bose-Einstein distribution.
</Definition>

## 2. Classical Johnson-Nyquist Noise

### The Fluctuation-Dissipation Theorem

The fluctuation-dissipation theorem connects thermal fluctuations to dissipation:

<Formula>
$$S_V(f) = 4k_BT \cdot R$$
</Formula>

This gives the voltage noise power spectral density across a resistor R at temperature T.

For current noise:

<Formula>
$$S_I(f) = \frac{4k_BT}{R}$$
</Formula>

<Tip>
Johnson-Nyquist noise is "white" noise - its power spectral density is independent of frequency in the classical regime. This changes at quantum frequencies where hf > kT.
</Tip>

### Noise Power

The total noise power delivered to a matched load:

<Formula>
$$P = k_BT \cdot B$$
</Formula>

where B is the bandwidth. This remarkably simple result shows that thermal noise power depends only on temperature and bandwidth, not on the resistance value.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Physical constants
kB = 1.381e-23  # J/K
h = 6.626e-34   # J*s

def classical_noise_psd(T, R):
    """Classical Johnson-Nyquist voltage noise PSD."""
    return 4 * kB * T * R

def quantum_noise_psd(f, T, R):
    """
    Quantum-corrected Johnson-Nyquist noise PSD.

    S_V(f) = 4R * (hf/2) * coth(hf/2kT)

    Reduces to classical result when hf << kT
    """
    x = h * f / (2 * kB * T)
    if isinstance(f, np.ndarray):
        result = np.zeros_like(f)
        # Avoid overflow in coth for large x
        mask = x < 100
        result[mask] = 4 * R * (h * f[mask] / 2) * (1/np.tanh(x[mask]))
        result[~mask] = 4 * R * (h * f[~mask] / 2)  # coth(x) -> 1 for large x
        return result
    else:
        if x > 100:
            return 4 * R * h * f / 2
        return 4 * R * (h * f / 2) * (1/np.tanh(x))

# Compare classical and quantum at different temperatures
f = np.logspace(6, 12, 200)  # 1 MHz to 1 THz
R = 50  # Ohms

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# Left: Noise PSD vs frequency
ax1 = axes[0]
for T, color in [(300, 'red'), (4, 'blue'), (0.020, 'green')]:
    S_classical = classical_noise_psd(T, R) * np.ones_like(f)
    S_quantum = quantum_noise_psd(f, T, R)

    ax1.loglog(f/1e9, S_classical * 1e18, '--', color=color, alpha=0.5)
    ax1.loglog(f/1e9, S_quantum * 1e18, '-', color=color,
               linewidth=2, label=f'T = {T} K')

ax1.axvline(x=5, color='gray', linestyle=':', label='Qubit freq (5 GHz)')
ax1.set_xlabel('Frequency (GHz)')
ax1.set_ylabel('S_V (aV^2/Hz)')
ax1.set_title('Johnson-Nyquist Noise: Classical (dashed) vs Quantum (solid)')
ax1.legend()
ax1.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

# Right: Thermal energy scale
ax2 = axes[1]
T_range = np.logspace(-2, 3, 100)
kT = kB * T_range
hf_5GHz = h * 5e9

ax2.loglog(T_range, kT / h / 1e9, 'b-', linewidth=2, label='k_B T / h')
ax2.axhline(y=5, color='r', linestyle='--', label='5 GHz qubit')
ax2.axvline(x=h * 5e9 / kB, color='gray', linestyle=':', alpha=0.5)
ax2.fill_between([0.01, h*5e9/kB], [0.01, 0.01], [1000, 1000],
                  alpha=0.2, color='green', label='Quantum regime')
ax2.set_xlabel('Temperature (K)')
ax2.set_ylabel('Frequency (GHz)')
ax2.set_title('Quantum vs Classical Regime')
ax2.set_xlim(0.01, 1000)
ax2.set_ylim(0.1, 1000)
ax2.legend()
ax2.grid(True, alpha=0.3)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

print(f"Crossover temperature for 5 GHz: T = hf/k = {h*5e9/kB*1000:.0f} mK")
```

## 3. Quantum Thermal Noise

### Bose-Einstein Distribution

At quantum frequencies ($h f > k_B T$), the thermal occupation of photon modes follows the Bose-Einstein distribution:

<Formula>
$$\bar{n}_{th} = \frac{1}{e^{h f / k_B T} - 1}$$
</Formula>

<Definition term="Thermal Photon Number">
The average number of thermal photons in a mode at frequency f and temperature T. At low temperatures (hf >> kT), this approaches zero exponentially, enabling quantum operation.
</Definition>

### Quantum Noise PSD

The quantum-corrected noise power spectral density is:

<Formula>
$$S_V(f) = 4R \cdot \frac{hf}{2} \cdot \coth\left(\frac{hf}{2k_BT}\right)$$
</Formula>

This reduces to:
- Classical limit ($hf \ll k_BT$): $S_V = 4k_BTR$
- Quantum limit ($hf \gg k_BT$): $S_V = 2hfR$ (zero-point fluctuations)

```python
import numpy as np

def thermal_photon_number(f_GHz, T_mK):
    """
    Calculate thermal photon number.

    Args:
        f_GHz: Frequency in GHz
        T_mK: Temperature in millikelvin

    Returns:
        Average thermal photon number
    """
    h = 6.626e-34
    kB = 1.381e-23
    f = f_GHz * 1e9
    T = T_mK * 1e-3

    x = h * f / (kB * T)
    if x > 100:
        return np.exp(-x)  # Avoid underflow
    return 1 / (np.exp(x) - 1)

# Calculate for typical qubit parameters
f_qubit = 5  # GHz

print("Thermal photon numbers for 5 GHz mode:")
print("-" * 40)
for T in [300e3, 4e3, 100, 50, 20, 10]:
    n_th = thermal_photon_number(f_qubit, T)
    print(f"T = {T:6.0f} mK: n_th = {n_th:.2e}")

# Critical temperature where n_th = 0.01
# hf/kT = ln(101) => T = hf/(k*ln(101))
T_crit = 6.626e-34 * 5e9 / (1.381e-23 * np.log(101)) * 1000
print(f"\nFor n_th < 0.01, need T < {T_crit:.0f} mK")
```

## 4. Thermal Noise Effects on Qubits

### Thermal Excitation

At finite temperature, the qubit has non-zero probability of being in the excited state:

<Formula>
$$P_1^{eq} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{\hbar\omega_{01}/k_BT}} \approx e^{-\hbar\omega_{01}/k_BT}$$
</Formula>

<Warning>
Even at 20 mK, a 5 GHz qubit has P1 ~ 0.1%. This seems small but compounds over many operations and can bias measurement results.
</Warning>

### Thermal T1 Enhancement

Thermal photons increase the effective decay rate:

<Formula>
$$\Gamma_1(T) = \Gamma_1^{sp}(1 + \bar{n}_{th}) + \Gamma_1^{abs} \cdot \bar{n}_{th}$$
</Formula>

where:
- $\Gamma_1^{sp}$ is the spontaneous emission rate
- $\Gamma_1^{abs}$ is the absorption rate
- $\bar{n}_{th}$ is the thermal photon number

For symmetric coupling: $\Gamma_1^{sp} = \Gamma_1^{abs} = \Gamma_1^0$, giving:

<Formula>
$$\Gamma_1(T) = \Gamma_1^0 (1 + 2\bar{n}_{th})$$
</Formula>

### Temperature Dependence of Coherence

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def T1_vs_temperature(T1_0_us, f_GHz, T_mK_array):
    """
    Calculate T1 vs temperature including thermal effects.

    Args:
        T1_0_us: Zero-temperature T1 in microseconds
        f_GHz: Qubit frequency in GHz
        T_mK_array: Array of temperatures in mK

    Returns:
        Array of T1 values in microseconds
    """
    T1_values = []

    for T_mK in T_mK_array:
        n_th = thermal_photon_number(f_GHz, T_mK)
        # T1 decreases with temperature
        T1 = T1_0_us / (1 + 2 * n_th)
        T1_values.append(T1)

    return np.array(T1_values)

# Calculate T1 vs temperature
T1_0 = 100  # us at T=0
f = 5  # GHz
T_range = np.logspace(0.5, 3, 100)  # 3 mK to 1000 mK

T1_values = T1_vs_temperature(T1_0, f, T_range)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.semilogx(T_range, T1_values, 'b-', linewidth=2)
plt.axhline(y=T1_0, color='gray', linestyle='--', label=f'T1(T=0) = {T1_0} us')
plt.axvline(x=20, color='red', linestyle=':', label='Typical operating T (20 mK)')
plt.xlabel('Temperature (mK)')
plt.ylabel('T1 (us)')
plt.title('T1 Degradation from Thermal Photons (5 GHz qubit)')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.xlim(3, 1000)
plt.show()

# Print key values
for T in [10, 20, 50, 100, 200]:
    T1 = T1_vs_temperature(T1_0, f, [T])[0]
    reduction = (T1_0 - T1) / T1_0 * 100
    print(f"T = {T:3d} mK: T1 = {T1:.1f} us ({reduction:.1f}% reduction)")
```

## 5. Thermal Photon Noise in Resonators

### Photon Shot Noise

Even in the ground state, vacuum fluctuations cause measurement uncertainty. At finite temperature, thermal photons add to this:

<Formula>
$$\sigma_n^2 = \bar{n}_{th}(\bar{n}_{th} + 1)$$
</Formula>

This is super-Poissonian due to the bosonic nature of photons.

### Resonator as Thermal Filter

A resonator acts as a frequency-selective thermal filter:

<Formula>
$$S_{out}(\omega) = |H(\omega)|^2 S_{in}(\omega)$$
</Formula>

where $H(\omega)$ is the resonator transfer function.

<Example>
Calculating thermal noise at the resonator output:

```python
import numpy as np

def resonator_transfer(f, f0, kappa):
    """
    Resonator transfer function.

    Args:
        f: Frequency
        f0: Resonance frequency
        kappa: Linewidth (FWHM)

    Returns:
        |H(f)|^2
    """
    delta = f - f0
    return (kappa/2)**2 / (delta**2 + (kappa/2)**2)

def thermal_noise_through_resonator(f0_GHz, kappa_MHz, T_mK, bandwidth_MHz):
    """
    Calculate integrated thermal noise power through a resonator.

    Returns noise in units of photon number.
    """
    f0 = f0_GHz * 1e9
    kappa = kappa_MHz * 1e6
    bandwidth = bandwidth_MHz * 1e6

    # Integrate over bandwidth
    f_range = np.linspace(f0 - bandwidth/2, f0 + bandwidth/2, 1000)
    df = f_range[1] - f_range[0]

    total_noise = 0
    for f in f_range:
        n_th = thermal_photon_number(f/1e9, T_mK)
        H_sq = resonator_transfer(f, f0, kappa)
        total_noise += n_th * H_sq * df

    # Normalize by resonator bandwidth
    return total_noise / kappa

# Example calculation
f0 = 7  # GHz
kappa = 1  # MHz
T = 50  # mK

n_filtered = thermal_noise_through_resonator(f0, kappa, T, 10)
n_unfiltered = thermal_photon_number(f0, T)

print(f"Unfiltered thermal photons at {f0} GHz, {T} mK: {n_unfiltered:.2e}")
print(f"After resonator filtering: {n_filtered:.2e}")
print(f"Filtering ratio: {n_unfiltered/n_filtered:.1f}x")
```
</Example>

## 6. Cryogenic Operation

### Why 20 mK?

The operating temperature of ~20 mK in dilution refrigerators is chosen to satisfy:

1. $k_BT \ll \hbar\omega_{01}$ (thermal photons negligible)
2. $k_BT \ll \Delta_{Al}$ (superconducting gap of aluminum ~200 mK)
3. Practical limit of dilution refrigeration technology

<Formula>
$$\frac{k_BT}{\hbar\omega_{01}} = \frac{(1.38 \times 10^{-23})(0.020)}{(1.05 \times 10^{-34})(2\pi \times 5 \times 10^9)} \approx 0.008$$
</Formula>

### Thermal Stages

A typical dilution refrigerator has multiple thermal stages:

| Stage | Temperature | Purpose |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 300 K | Room temp | Electronics |
| 50 K | First stage | Radiation shield |
| 4 K | Pulse tube | Microwave components |
| 1 K | Still | Thermal anchoring |
| 100 mK | Cold plate | Intermediate |
| 20 mK | Mixing chamber | Qubit operation |

## 7. Thermal Filtering Strategies

### Attenuation at Each Stage

Microwave signals must be attenuated at each temperature stage to prevent hot photons from reaching the qubit:

<Formula>
$$n_{eff} = \sum_i n_{th}(T_i) \cdot \prod_{j>i} A_j$$
</Formula>

where $A_j$ is the attenuation factor at stage j.

<Tip>
A common strategy is 20 dB attenuation at 4K, 10 dB at 100 mK, and 10 dB at 20 mK. This reduces 4K thermal noise by 10^4, bringing it below the quantum limit.
</Tip>

### IR Filtering

Infrared radiation can excite quasiparticles even if microwaves are well-filtered:

- Use light-tight packaging
- Eccosorb or similar absorbers
- Multi-layer shielding
- Low-pass filters with IR-absorbing substrates

## 8. Summary

Thermal noise fundamentally limits quantum coherence:

1. **Classical regime**: $S_V = 4k_BTR$ (white noise)
2. **Quantum regime**: Bose-Einstein distribution determines photon number
3. **Critical temperature**: $T < hf/k_B$ for quantum operation
4. **T1 degradation**: Factor of $(1 + 2\bar{n}_{th})$ enhancement
5. **Cryogenic operation**: 20 mK achieves $\bar{n}_{th} < 10^{-3}$

Key numbers to remember:
- 5 GHz qubit at 20 mK: $\bar{n}_{th} \approx 10^{-5}$
- 5 GHz qubit at 50 mK: $\bar{n}_{th} \approx 10^{-2}$
- Need T < 50 mK for negligible thermal excitation

<Warning>
Even with perfect filtering, other temperature-dependent mechanisms (quasiparticles, TLS) often dominate at millikelvin temperatures. Thermal photon noise sets a fundamental floor, but achieving it requires addressing all noise sources.
</Warning>
