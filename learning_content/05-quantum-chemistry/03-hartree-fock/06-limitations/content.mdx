## 1. The Mean-Field Approximation

<Definition term="Mean-Field Approximation">
The central approximation in Hartree-Fock where each electron moves in the average field created by all other electrons, ignoring instantaneous electron-electron interactions.
</Definition>

```python
def mean_field_limitation():
    """Explain the mean-field approximation and its consequences."""

    print("Mean-Field Approximation in HF")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nWhat HF Assumes:")
    print("  • Each electron feels average field of others")
    print("  • No instantaneous e-e correlation")
    print("  • Electrons are independent particles in effective potential")

    print("\nMathematically:")
    print("  Ψ_HF = single Slater determinant")
    print("  V_eff(r₁) = ∫ ρ(r₂)/|r₁-r₂| dr₂  (average)")
    print()
    print("  Reality: V(r₁,r₂) depends on BOTH positions simultaneously")

    print("\nConsequence:")
    print("  • Electrons get too close to each other")
    print("  • e-e repulsion overestimated on average")
    print("  • Energy is too HIGH (variational principle)")

    print("\nThe Missing Physics:")
    print("  • Coulomb hole: electrons avoid each other")
    print("  • Dynamic correlation: instantaneous avoidance")
    print("  • Static correlation: near-degeneracy effects")

mean_field_limitation()
```

## 2. Electron Correlation

<Formula>
$$E_{corr} = E_{exact} - E_{HF}$$
</Formula>

```python
def electron_correlation():
    """Explain electron correlation energy."""

    print("Electron Correlation Energy")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nDefinition (Löwdin, 1959):")
    print("  E_corr = E_exact - E_HF")
    print()
    print("  Always NEGATIVE (HF energy is always too high)")

    print("\nTypes of Correlation:")
    print("-" * 45)

    print("\n1. Dynamic Correlation:")
    print("   • Short-range electron avoidance")
    print("   • 'Coulomb hole' around each electron")
    print("   • Recovered by CI, MP2, CC methods")
    print("   • Present in ALL systems")

    print("\n2. Static (Non-dynamic) Correlation:")
    print("   • Near-degeneracy of configurations")
    print("   • Bond breaking, diradicals, transition metals")
    print("   • Requires multi-reference methods")
    print("   • HF qualitatively wrong here!")

    print("\nTypical Correlation Energies:")
    molecules = [
        ('He', -0.042, 1.5),
        ('H₂', -0.041, 3.6),
        ('H₂O', -0.37, 1.0),
        ('N₂', -0.55, 1.5),
        ('Benzene', -1.5, 0.6),
    ]

    print(f"\n{'Molecule':<12} {'E_corr (Eh)':<15} {'% of E_total':<12}")
    print("-" * 40)
    for mol, ecorr, pct in molecules:
        print(f"{mol:<12} {ecorr:<15.3f} {pct:<12.1f}")

    print("\n  Note: Only ~1% of total energy, but crucial for chemistry!")

electron_correlation()
```

<Warning>
Although correlation energy is only ~1% of total energy, it's often 100% of the chemically relevant energy differences like bond energies and reaction barriers.
</Warning>

## 3. Bond Dissociation Problem

<Definition term="Dissociation Catastrophe">
The qualitative failure of restricted Hartree-Fock (RHF) to correctly describe bond breaking, leading to incorrect asymptotic energies and unphysical ionic character at large separations.
</Definition>

```python
import numpy as np

def dissociation_problem():
    """Illustrate HF failure for bond dissociation."""

    print("HF Bond Dissociation Problem")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nExample: H₂ Dissociation")
    print("-" * 45)

    print("\nRHF Wavefunction:")
    print("  Ψ_RHF = |σ_g↑ σ_g↓⟩")
    print("  σ_g = (1sₐ + 1sᵦ)/√2  (bonding orbital)")

    print("\nExpanding at large R:")
    print("  Ψ_RHF → ½|1sₐ↑1sₐ↓⟩ + ½|1sₐ↑1sᵦ↓⟩ + ½|1sᵦ↑1sₐ↓⟩ + ½|1sᵦ↑1sᵦ↓⟩")
    print()
    print("  = 50% (H• + H•) + 50% (H⁺ + H⁻)")
    print()
    print("  WRONG! Should be 100% neutral atoms!")

    print("\nCorrect Dissociation:")
    print("  H₂ → H• + H•  (two neutral atoms)")
    print("  Requires two configurations: σ_g² and σ_u²")

    print("\nEnergy Error at Dissociation:")
    print("  RHF: E → 2E(H) + J₁ₛ₁ₛ ≈ 0.3 Eh TOO HIGH")
    print("  Exact: E → 2E(H)")

    # Illustrative data
    print("\nPotential Energy Curves (schematic):")
    print(f"{'R (Å)':<10} {'E_RHF (Eh)':<15} {'E_exact (Eh)':<15} {'Error':<10}")
    print("-" * 50)
    data = [
        (0.74, -1.133, -1.174, 0.041),
        (1.5, -0.98, -1.05, 0.07),
        (3.0, -0.85, -1.00, 0.15),
        (5.0, -0.72, -1.00, 0.28),
        ('∞', -0.70, -1.00, 0.30),
    ]
    for r, ehf, eex, err in data:
        print(f"{str(r):<10} {ehf:<15.3f} {eex:<15.3f} {err:<10.2f}")

dissociation_problem()
```

<Tip>
If your HF calculation shows spin contamination (⟨S²⟩ ≠ S(S+1)), it's a strong indicator of static correlation problems.
</Tip>

## 6. When HF Is Still Useful

```python
def when_hf_useful():
    """Cases where HF remains valuable."""

    print("When Hartree-Fock Is Appropriate")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nHF Works Well For:")
    print("-" * 45)

    good_cases = [
        ('Equilibrium geometries', 'Bond lengths within 0.01-0.02 Å'),
        ('Molecular vibrations', 'After scaling, ~3% error'),
        ('Dipole moments', 'Within 5-10% for polar molecules'),
        ('Relative conformations', 'Energy ordering usually correct'),
        ('Orbital analysis', 'Qualitative MO pictures'),
        ('Starting point', 'Reference for post-HF methods'),
    ]

    for case, accuracy in good_cases:
        print(f"  • {case}: {accuracy}")

    print("\nHF as Reference:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("  • MP2, CCSD, etc. start from HF orbitals")
    print("  • HF captures ~99% of total energy")
    print("  • Orbital shapes are qualitatively correct")
    print("  • Fast enough for large systems")

    print("\nRule of Thumb:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("  Use HF when:")
    print("    ✓ All electrons are paired (closed-shell)")
    print("    ✓ No bond breaking or making")
    print("    ✓ No transition metals")
    print("    ✓ Structure, not energetics, is the goal")
    print()
    print("  Avoid HF when:")
    print("    ✗ Computing reaction energies")
    print("    ✗ Studying bond dissociation")
    print("    ✗ Modeling weak interactions")
    print("    ✗ Transition states or radicals")

when_hf_useful()
```

## Summary

In this lesson, you learned:

1. **Mean-field approximation** ignores instantaneous electron correlation
2. **Correlation energy** is small (~1%) but chemically crucial
3. **Bond dissociation** fails qualitatively in RHF
4. **Static correlation** requires multireference methods
5. **Property errors** vary: structures good, energies poor
6. **HF remains useful** as reference and for structures
7. **Post-HF methods** systematically improve accuracy

Understanding HF limitations is essential for choosing appropriate methods.
