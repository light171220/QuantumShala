## 1. The Meaning of Orbital Energies

The Fock eigenvalue equation gives orbital energies:

<Formula>
$$\hat{f}|\chi_i\rangle = \varepsilon_i|\chi_i\rangle$$
</Formula>

```python
def orbital_energy_meaning():
    """Explain what orbital energies represent."""

    print("Physical Meaning of Orbital Energies")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nFormal Definition:")
    print("  εᵢ = ⟨χᵢ|f̂|χᵢ⟩")
    print("     = hᵢᵢ + Σⱼ(Jᵢⱼ - Kᵢⱼ)")

    print("\nBreaking This Down:")
    print("  εᵢ = Kinetic energy of electron in orbital i")
    print("     + Nuclear attraction")
    print("     + Coulomb repulsion from OTHER electrons")
    print("     - Exchange stabilization with same-spin electrons")

    print("\nImportant Notes:")
    print("  • εᵢ is NOT the total energy contribution of orbital i")
    print("  • E_HF ≠ Σᵢ εᵢ  (would double count e-e repulsion)")
    print("  • Correct: E_HF = Σᵢ εᵢ - ½Σᵢⱼ(Jᵢⱼ - Kᵢⱼ) + V_nn")

    print("\nTypical Values:")
    examples = [
        ('Core 1s (C)', -11.3, 'Very tightly bound'),
        ('Core 1s (O)', -20.7, 'More tightly bound'),
        ('Valence 2p (C)', -0.4, 'Involved in bonding'),
        ('HOMO (typical)', -0.3, 'Highest occupied'),
        ('LUMO (typical)', 0.1, 'Lowest unoccupied'),
    ]

    print(f"\n{'Orbital':<18} {'ε (Eh)':<12} {'Character':<20}")
    print("-" * 50)
    for orb, energy, character in examples:
        print(f"{orb:<18} {energy:<12.1f} {character:<20}")

orbital_energy_meaning()
```

## 2. Koopmans' Theorem

<Definition term="Koopmans' Theorem">
The first ionization potential equals the negative of the HOMO energy: IP ≈ -ε_HOMO. Similarly, the electron affinity approximately equals the negative of the LUMO energy: EA ≈ -ε_LUMO.
</Definition>

<Formula>
$$IP \approx -\varepsilon_{HOMO}, \quad EA \approx -\varepsilon_{LUMO}$$
</Formula>

```python
def koopmans_theorem():
    """Explain and demonstrate Koopmans' theorem."""

    print("Koopmans' Theorem")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nStatement:")
    print("  IP ≈ -ε_HOMO  (ionization potential)")
    print("  EA ≈ -ε_LUMO  (electron affinity)")

    print("\nDerivation (IP):")
    print("  E(N-1) - E(N) = ?")
    print()
    print("  Assume 'frozen orbitals': removing electron doesn't")
    print("  change other orbitals (no relaxation).")
    print()
    print("  Then:")
    print("  E(N-1) - E(N) = -εᵢ for removing from orbital i")
    print()
    print("  For HOMO: IP ≈ -ε_HOMO")

    print("\nComparison with Experiment:")
    data = [
        ('H₂O', 0.500, 12.6, 12.4, 1.6),
        ('NH₃', 0.414, 11.1, 10.2, 8.8),
        ('CH₄', 0.543, 14.4, 14.0, 2.9),
        ('CO', 0.554, 14.0, 13.1, 6.4),
    ]

    print(f"\n{'Molecule':<10} {'−ε_HOMO':<10} {'Koop IP':<10} {'Exp IP':<10} {'Error %':<10}")
    print("-" * 55)
    for mol, homo, koop, exp, err in data:
        print(f"{mol:<10} {homo:<10.3f} {koop:<10.1f} {exp:<10.1f} {err:<10.1f}")

koopmans_theorem()
```

<Tip>
Koopmans' theorem provides quick estimates of ionization potentials without computing the ion. However, errors of 1-2 eV are common due to orbital relaxation in the ion.
</Tip>

## 3. Limitations of Koopmans' Theorem

```python
def koopmans_limitations():
    """Discuss when Koopmans' theorem fails."""

    print("Limitations of Koopmans' Theorem")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\n1. ORBITAL RELAXATION:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("   Assumption: Orbitals don't change upon ionization")
    print("   Reality: They relax to accommodate missing electron")
    print("   Effect: Koopmans overestimates IP by ~1-2 eV")

    print("\n2. ELECTRON CORRELATION:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("   Assumption: HF is exact")
    print("   Reality: Missing correlation energy differs N vs N-1")
    print("   Effect: Can increase or decrease IP error")

    print("\n3. RELATIVISTIC EFFECTS:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("   For heavy elements, relativistic contraction of")
    print("   core orbitals significantly affects energies")

    print("\n4. MULTI-REFERENCE CHARACTER:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("   If the ion has multiple important configurations,")
    print("   HF may give a qualitatively wrong description")

    print("\nError Cancellation:")
    print("  Sometimes relaxation and correlation errors cancel!")
    print("  Koopmans can work 'by accident' for some systems")

koopmans_limitations()
```

## 4. The HOMO-LUMO Gap

<Definition term="HOMO-LUMO Gap">
The energy difference between the Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital and the Lowest Unoccupied Molecular Orbital: Δ = ε_LUMO - ε_HOMO.
</Definition>

```python
def homo_lumo_gap():
    """Discuss the HOMO-LUMO gap and its significance."""

    print("The HOMO-LUMO Gap")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nDefinition:")
    print("  Δ = ε_LUMO - ε_HOMO")

    print("\nPhysical Significance:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("  • Related to chemical stability")
    print("  • Approximates optical gap (very crudely)")
    print("  • Larger gap → less reactive, more 'stable'")
    print("  • Smaller gap → more reactive, colored compounds")

    print("\nExamples:")
    compounds = [
        ('Benzene', 9.2, 'Stable, UV absorption'),
        ('Polyacetylene (long)', 1.5, 'Conducting, colored'),
        ('Diamond', 5.5, 'Insulator, colorless'),
        ('Graphene', 0.0, 'Conductor'),
        ('Transition metal complex', 2.0, 'Colored, reactive'),
    ]

    print(f"\n{'Compound':<25} {'Gap (eV)':<12} {'Properties':<25}")
    print("-" * 60)
    for comp, gap, props in compounds:
        print(f"{comp:<25} {gap:<12.1f} {props:<25}")

    print("\nWarning:")
    print("  HF HOMO-LUMO gap is NOT the optical gap!")
    print("  Optical gap requires excited state calculation")
    print("  (TDDFT, EOM-CC, etc.)")

homo_lumo_gap()
```

```python
    print("  ↑")

            spacer = "  "
        else:
            spacer = ""
        print(f"  │ {energy:>7.2f} {bar} {name:<15} {character}")

            print("  │")
            print("  │ - - - - - - - - - HOMO-LUMO Gap - - - - - - - -")
            print("  │")

    print("  │")
    print("  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────→")
```

## 7. Connection to Reactivity

```python
def orbital_reactivity():
    """Connect orbital energies to chemical reactivity."""

    print("Orbital Energies and Reactivity")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nFrontier Molecular Orbital Theory:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("  HOMO: Where electrons are donated FROM")
    print("  LUMO: Where electrons are accepted TO")
    print()
    print("  HOMO of nucleophile → LUMO of electrophile")

    print("\nHardness and Softness:")
    print("-" * 45)
    print("  Chemical hardness η = (ε_LUMO - ε_HOMO) / 2")
    print("  Chemical softness S = 1 / η")
    print()
    print("  Hard: Large gap, localized, ionic")
    print("  Soft: Small gap, diffuse, covalent")

    print("\nApplications:")
    examples = [
        ('Nucleophilicity', 'High HOMO energy', 'Good e⁻ donor'),
        ('Electrophilicity', 'Low LUMO energy', 'Good e⁻ acceptor'),
        ('Radical stability', 'SOMO energy', 'Half-filled orbital'),
        ('Conductivity', 'Small gap', 'Electrons easily excited'),
```

## Summary

In this lesson, you learned:

1. **Orbital energies** represent one-electron energies in the mean field
2. **Koopmans' theorem** relates ε to ionization potentials
3. **Orbital relaxation** causes Koopmans to overestimate IP
4. **HOMO-LUMO gap** indicates stability and reactivity
5. **Symmetry labels** classify orbitals by point group
6. **Frontier orbitals** govern chemical reactivity
7. **Post-HF methods** refine orbital energy meaning

Orbital energies are central to understanding molecular electronic structure.
