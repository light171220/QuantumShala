## 1. The Central Equation of Quantum Chemistry

All of quantum chemistry begins with one equation:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H}\Psi = E\Psi$$
</Formula>

<Definition term="Schrödinger Equation">
The fundamental equation of quantum mechanics that describes how the quantum state of a physical system evolves. The time-independent form gives the stationary states and their energies.
</Definition>

For molecules, solving this equation tells us:
- Electronic structure and bonding
- Molecular geometry
- Spectroscopic properties
- Chemical reactivity

<Tip>
The Schrödinger equation is to quantum chemistry what F=ma is to classical mechanics—the starting point for understanding everything.
</Tip>

## 2. The Molecular Hamiltonian

The Hamiltonian operator contains all the physics of the molecular system:

<Formula>
$$\hat{H} = \hat{T}_n + \hat{T}_e + \hat{V}_{nn} + \hat{V}_{ee} + \hat{V}_{ne}$$
</Formula>

<Definition term="Molecular Hamiltonian">
The quantum mechanical operator representing the total energy of a molecular system, including kinetic energy of nuclei and electrons, and all Coulombic interactions.
</Definition>

### Term-by-Term Breakdown

```python
def molecular_hamiltonian_terms():
```

## 3. The Full Molecular Schrödinger Equation

In atomic units (ℏ = m_e = e = 4πε₀ = 1):

<Formula>
$$\left[-\sum_{A=1}^{M}\frac{1}{2M_A}\nabla^2_A - \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2_i + \sum_{A<B}\frac{Z_AZ_B}{R_{AB}} + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}} - \sum_{A,i}\frac{Z_A}{r_{Ai}}\right]\Psi = E\Psi$$
</Formula>

where:
- M = number of nuclei, N = number of electrons
- $R_{AB}$ = distance between nuclei A and B
- $r_{ij}$ = distance between electrons i and j
- $r_{Ai}$ = distance between nucleus A and electron i
- $Z_A$ = atomic number of nucleus A

```python
import numpy as np

def count_interaction_terms(n_nuclei, n_electrons):
    """Count the number of interaction terms in the Hamiltonian."""

    # Kinetic energy terms
    T_nuclear = n_nuclei
    T_electronic = n_electrons

    # Coulomb interaction terms
    V_nn = n_nuclei * (n_nuclei - 1) // 2
    V_ee = n_electrons * (n_electrons - 1) // 2
    V_ne = n_nuclei * n_electrons

    total = T_nuclear + T_electronic + V_nn + V_ee + V_ne

    print(f"Hamiltonian for {n_nuclei} nuclei and {n_electrons} electrons:")
    print(f"  Nuclear kinetic terms: {T_nuclear}")
    print(f"  Electronic kinetic terms: {T_electronic}")
    print(f"  Nuclear-nuclear repulsions: {V_nn}")
    print(f"  Electron-electron repulsions: {V_ee}")
    print(f"  Nuclear-electron attractions: {V_ne}")
    print(f"  Total terms: {total}")

    return total

# Examples
molecules = [
    ('H₂', 2, 2),
    ('H₂O', 3, 10),
    ('Benzene', 12, 42),
    ('Hemoglobin', 9000, 25000)  # Approximate
]

print("Hamiltonian Complexity")
print("=" * 60)
for name, nuclei, electrons in molecules:
    print(f"\n{name}:")
    count_interaction_terms(nuclei, electrons)
```

## 4. Why Exact Solutions Are Impossible

<Definition term="Many-Body Problem">
The challenge of solving the Schrödinger equation for systems with more than one electron. The electron-electron repulsion term couples all electrons, preventing separation of variables.
</Definition>

### The Electron-Electron Problem

The troublemaker is $V_{ee}$:

<Formula>
$$V_{ee} = \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}}$$
</Formula>

This term:
- Couples all electrons together
- Prevents separation of variables
- Creates the "correlation" problem
- Makes the wavefunction a function of all electron coordinates simultaneously

```python
def demonstrate_inseparability():
    """Show why V_ee prevents separation."""

    print("Why Electron-Electron Repulsion Makes Things Hard")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nFor a 2-electron system, the wavefunction is:")
    print("  Ψ(r₁, r₂)")
    print()
    print("If we could ignore V_ee, we could write:")
    print("  Ψ(r₁, r₂) = φ(r₁) × χ(r₂)  [separable!]")
    print()
    print("But V_ee = 1/|r₁ - r₂| depends on BOTH r₁ AND r₂")
    print("This couples the electrons: Ψ cannot be factored!")
    print()
    print("The wavefunction dimensionality:")

    for n_electrons in [2, 4, 10, 20, 50]:
        # Each electron has 3 spatial coordinates (x, y, z)
        dimensions = 3 * n_electrons
        # Plus 1 spin coordinate each
        spin_configs = 2 ** n_electrons
        print(f"  {n_electrons:2} electrons: {dimensions}D spatial × {spin_configs} spin configurations")

demonstrate_inseparability()
```

<Warning>
The wavefunction for N electrons is a function of 3N spatial coordinates plus spin. This "curse of dimensionality" is why classical computers struggle with quantum chemistry.
</Warning>

## 5. Energy Components and the Virial Theorem

At equilibrium geometry, the virial theorem relates kinetic and potential energy:

<Formula>
$$2\langle T \rangle = -\langle V \rangle$$
$$E = \langle T \rangle + \langle V \rangle = -\langle T \rangle = \frac{1}{2}\langle V \rangle$$
</Formula>

```python
def energy_decomposition(E_total, T_kinetic):
    """
    Decompose total energy using virial theorem.
    At equilibrium: 2T = -V, so V = -2T and E = T + V = -T
    """
    V_potential = E_total - T_kinetic

    print("Energy Decomposition (Virial Theorem)")
    print("=" * 50)
    print(f"Total Energy E: {E_total:.6f} Hartree")
    print(f"Kinetic Energy T: {T_kinetic:.6f} Hartree")
    print(f"Potential Energy V: {V_potential:.6f} Hartree")
    print()
    print(f"Check virial theorem (at equilibrium):")
    print(f"  2T = {2*T_kinetic:.6f}")
    print(f"  -V = {-V_potential:.6f}")
    print(f"  Ratio 2T/(-V) = {2*T_kinetic/(-V_potential):.4f} (should be ≈1.0)")

# Example: H₂ molecule at equilibrium
E_H2 = -1.174  # Hartree
T_H2 = 1.174   # By virial theorem, T = -E at equilibrium

energy_decomposition(E_H2, T_H2)
```

## 6. Connection to Quantum Computing

The molecular Schrödinger equation is where quantum computers shine:

<Definition term="Quantum Advantage in Chemistry">
The potential for quantum computers to solve the electronic Schrödinger equation exponentially faster than classical computers for certain problems, particularly for strongly correlated systems.
</Definition>

```python
def quantum_computing_advantage():
    """Explain where quantum computers help."""

    classical_limits = {
        'Full CI': {
            'what': 'Exact solution within basis',
            'scaling': 'Exponential O(M^N)',
            'limit': '~18 electrons classically'
        },
        'CCSD(T)': {
            'what': 'Gold standard approximation',
            'scaling': 'O(N⁷)',
            'limit': '~50 atoms classically'
        }
    }

    quantum_methods = {
        'VQE': {
            'what': 'Variational quantum eigensolver',
            'scaling': 'Polynomial in qubits',
            'advantage': 'Near-term feasible'
        },
        'QPE': {
            'what': 'Quantum phase estimation',
            'scaling': 'Polynomial with error',
            'advantage': 'Exponential speedup for FCI'
        }
    }

    print("Classical vs Quantum Approaches")
    print("=" * 60)

    print("\nClassical Limitations:")
    for method, info in classical_limits.items():
        print(f"\n  {method}:")
        print(f"    What: {info['what']}")
        print(f"    Scaling: {info['scaling']}")
        print(f"    Practical limit: {info['limit']}")

    print("\n" + "-"*60)
    print("\nQuantum Approaches:")
    for method, info in quantum_methods.items():
        print(f"\n  {method}:")
        print(f"    What: {info['what']}")
        print(f"    Scaling: {info['scaling']}")
        print(f"    Advantage: {info['advantage']}")

quantum_computing_advantage()
```

## 7. Atomic Units

Quantum chemists use atomic units to simplify equations:

<Formula>
$$\hbar = m_e = e = 4\pi\epsilon_0 = 1$$
</Formula>

| Quantity | Atomic Unit | SI Value |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Length | Bohr (a₀) | 5.292 × 10⁻¹¹ m |
| Energy | Hartree (Eh) | 4.360 × 10⁻¹⁸ J = 27.211 eV |
| Mass | Electron mass | 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg |
| Charge | Elementary charge | 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C |
| Time | ℏ/Eh | 2.419 × 10⁻¹⁷ s |

```python
def unit_conversions():
    """Common unit conversions in quantum chemistry."""

    conversions = {
        'Hartree → eV': 27.2114,
        'Hartree → kJ/mol': 2625.50,
        'Hartree → kcal/mol': 627.509,
        'Bohr → Ångstrom': 0.529177,
        'Hartree → cm⁻¹': 219474.6,
    }

    print("Unit Conversions (Atomic Units)")
    print("=" * 40)
    for conversion, factor in conversions.items():
        print(f"  1 {conversion}: {factor}")

    # Example calculation
    print("\nExample: H₂ bond energy")
    E_hartree = 0.1745
    print(f"  {E_hartree} Hartree")
    print(f"  = {E_hartree * 27.2114:.2f} eV")
    print(f"  = {E_hartree * 627.509:.1f} kcal/mol")

unit_conversions()
```
